Part A — Theory

A1. The Problem: DP Needs a Model You Don't Have

In Sessions 3-4, every Bellman update assumed you could write down p(s', r | s, a) — the exact probability of landing in state s' with reward r, for every state-action pair. Dynamic Programming (DP) used this model directly: Policy Evaluation and Policy Iteration are nothing but repeated sweeps over this known model.

Real environments don't hand you that. Prof. Sangeetha's framing:

"In Reinforcement Learning, model is the information that I have about the world around me — the model dynamics. ... You will not get the complete model dynamics in real world always, right? So this will not be available to you always." — Prof. Sangeetha, cs4_1 Transcript §3:50

Her anchor analogy is a warehouse pick-and-place robot — an Autonomous Mobile Robot (AMR):

"Real-world environments are completely dynamic — objects may or may not be where you expect, people and things come and go. You will not get the complete model dynamics in real world always." — Prof. Sangeetha, cs4_1 Transcript §3:44–6:11

Consequence for the Bellman equation: the term V(s) = 𝔼[r + γV(s')] requires averaging over p(s',r|s,a) — and that piece is simply not available.

In English: you still know your states, your actions, and you can observe the reward after you act — but you cannot enumerate "if I do this, here's the probability of every possible outcome." DP's update formula literally cannot be evaluated.

A2. The Fix: Stop Modelling, Start Sampling

Prof. Sangeetha's bridge into Monte Carlo (MC) methods uses a classic statistics analogy — estimating the average height of 10,000 students in a school:

"You can't measure everyone... so you use a sampling-based approach. Monte Carlo methods are good sampling-based estimation methods. ... In the case of Reinforcement Learning, you do not know what the actual model is here, so we are thinking about going for a sampling-based [estimation]." — Prof. Sangeetha, cs4_1 Transcript §6:22–6:68

Just as you'd measure a few hundred students and average their heights instead of measuring all 10,000, in RL you don't need p(s',r|s,a) if you can instead play out episodes and average what actually happened.

What gets "sampled" in RL? A set of interactions — a SARS′-type sequence, ending at a terminal state. One full sequence is called an episode or trajectory (τ); one (S,A,R,S′) tuple within it is one sample.

In English: "samples = episodes/trajectories." Instead of asking a model "what would happen if...", you actually act, watch what happens, record the reward sequence, and repeat across many episodes — then average.

A4. Backup Diagrams — BFS vs DFS

Prof. Sangeetha's framing of the structural difference, using classical search algorithms:

"This dynamic programming, I can equate it to the classical searches — one level of breadth-first search. ... Monte Carlo is just exploring, going deeper and deeper. It's more like performing depth-first search. ... In dynamic programming, you do bootstrapping — but in Monte Carlo, the next state alone is not going to help you. You have to finish the complete episode." — Prof. Sangeetha, cs4_1 Transcript §95–19:23

DP backup — shallow, wide (BFS-like, one-step lookahead)

         ／ │ ＼          all actions a ∈ A(s)
       a1   a2   a3
      ／│   │ ＼  │＼      all (s′,r) weighted by p(s′,r|s,a)
    s′ s′  s′  s′ s′ s′
   (V(s′) estimates used at every leaf — bootstrapping)

In English: from s, fan out across every action and every possible next state/reward, each weighted by its known probability, and combine using the current value estimates V(s′) of the leaves. One "sweep" updates V(s) using this whole shallow tree.

            │
            a   (action actually taken)
            │
            s′
            │
            a′
            │
            s″
            │
            ⋮
            │

   (no branching — just the ONE trajectory that happened;

In English: from s, follow the single sequence of actions/states/rewards that actually occurred, all the way to the terminal state T. There's "no max operator," no branching — just one path. Only after reaching T can you compute Gt, the real return, and use it to update V(s).

A5. The PE-PI Cycle Carries Over — But Starts From Nothing

Recall from cs3 that Policy Iteration alternates Policy Evaluation (find Vπ for the current π) and Policy Improvement (make π greedier w.r.t. that V). MC keeps this same two-step rhythm:

"The same PEPI cycle is what is going to happen in Monte Carlo also — but with the thought that you are starting off with a random policy. ... I don't have a model at all. I have to just randomly start my interaction with the environment, which in other words is a policy. ... Then I start evaluating the values of the states or actions based on that policy. Then I go towards improving." — Prof. Sangeetha, cs4_1 Transcript §28:51–32:00

A student (Rudresh R) asked directly whether MC is "pure exploration" — the prof's answer is the key framing for the whole topic:

"No, no, no, no. There is a mix of exploration and exploitation. ... You start off definitely with randomness here. There is no deterministic thoughts; you start the problem with random thoughts." — Prof. Sangeetha, cs4_1 Transcript §24:12–25:05

In English: MC begins from an arbitrary (random) policy — pure exploration at the start, because there's no model to be smart with. The PE step (averaging returns) and PI step (improving the policy toward the actions with higher estimated value) gradually shift the balance toward exploitation. This same arc — start random, evaluate, improve, repeat — is exactly the PE-PI cycle from Policy Iteration, just without the probability-weighted Bellman update.

Prof annotation (Slide 5): "New twist: don't know the model and the reward function" — formally, this is the moment the course shifts from model-based DP (Sessions 3-4) to model-free MC (this session). Everything that follows in Sections 1-4 assumes you know S, A, and γ, but must learn p(s',r|s,a) and vπ/qπ purely from episodes.

Why This Matters

Monte Carlo is the gateway to model-free RL. Every algorithm later in this course — TD learning, Q-learning, SARSA, and ultimately Deep RL methods like DQN — is built on the same core idea introduced here: learn value functions from sampled experience instead of a known model. MC is the "purest" version of this idea (no bootstrapping at all); TD methods will later show how to get some of DP's efficiency back without giving up the model-free property.

S&B Canonical

Formally, MC estimates vπ(s) = 𝔼π[Gt | St=s] by drawing many i.i.d. samples of Gt (one first-visit return per episode in which s occurs) and averaging — the textbook Law-of-Large-Numbers estimator. Because the samples are i.i.d., the convergence story is clean: the sample mean converges almost surely to vπ(s), and the estimator is unbiased for every finite number of episodes M. The price is the 1/√M convergence rate — to halve your error, you need 4× the episodes. (Sutton & Barto 2nd ed, Ch.5)

Self-check: A DP sweep updates V(s) using V(s′) for every possible successor s′, weighted by p(s′|s,a). An MC update uses... what, exactly — and why can't it be computed until the episode ends?

Click to reveal answer

An MC update uses the actual return Gt — the real sum of (discounted) rewards observed from time t until the episode terminates. It can't be computed mid-episode because Gt = Rt+1 + γGt+1 depends on all future rewards, which aren't known until the trajectory actually reaches a terminal state T. There is no V(s′) "shortcut" (no bootstrapping) — you must wait for the whole episode.

Part B — Worked Examples: Sampling vs. Modelling

These examples are about the concept of MC — "average what you observed" vs "compute the expectation from known probabilities" — using the dice/coin-flip style intuition before we touch RL-specific notation (that comes in Section 2).

Easy: Estimating the Average Dice Roll — Two Ways

You want to know the average value of a fair six-sided die roll.

  1. The "model" way (like DP): you know the model — each face 1,2,3,4,5,6 has probability 1/6. Compute the expectation directly: 𝔼[X] = Σ x·p(x) = (1+2+3+4+5+6)/6.
  2. This gives exactly 3.5 — no randomness, no error, computed once and forever (as long as the die is truly fair and you trust the model).
  3. The "sampling" way (like MC): you do NOT assume you know the probabilities. Instead, roll the die N times and record each outcome.
  4. Average the observed outcomes: V ≈ (1/N) Σ xi. For N=10 rolls giving 4,2,6,3,5,1,6,2,4,3 → average = 36/10 = 3.6.
  5. As N grows (100, 1000, 10000 rolls), this sample average gets closer and closer to 3.5 — the true expectation.
Answer: Model-based expectation = 3.5 (exact). Sample-based average from 10 rolls = 3.6 (close, but noisy). With more rolls, the sample average converges to 3.5.
Key Insight: the "model way" needed you to know p(x) for every face in advance. The "sampling way" needed nothing but the ability to roll the die and observe the result — exactly like MC needing only the ability to run episodes, not p(s',r|s,a).

Medium: Applying This to a 1-State MDP

Now put this in an RL frame. Suppose a 1-state MDP: every step, the agent is in state s and takes the only available action "stay." Each step gives a random reward — think of it as "rolling a die" for the reward: equally likely to be +1, +2, +3, +4, +5, +6 — and the episode always lasts exactly 1 step before terminating (γ doesn't matter here since there's only one reward).

  1. DP-style (model known): if you knew the reward distribution p(r|s,a) = 1/6 for each of r ∈ {1,...,6}, you could compute Vπ(s) = 𝔼[R] = 3.5 directly — one calculation, exact, done.
  2. MC-style (model unknown): you don't know p(r|s,a). All you can do is run episodes — each episode = "agent is in s, takes action, observes reward, episode ends."
  3. Episode 1: observed return G1 = +4. Episode 2: G2 = +2. Episode 3: G3 = +6. Episode 4: G4 = +1. Episode 5: G5 = +3.
  4. First-visit MC estimate (s is visited exactly once per episode here, so first-visit = every-visit in this special case): V(s) ≈ (4+2+6+1+3)/5 = 16/5 = 3.2.
  5. Run 5 more episodes and the running average will drift closer to 3.5 — without ever needing to know the reward probabilities.
Answer: V(s) ≈ 3.2 after 5 episodes (true value 3.5). The MC estimate is unbiased — its expected value across many runs of "5 episodes" is exactly 3.5, but any single run of 5 episodes has sampling error.
Key Insight: V(s) here is computed exactly the same way as the dice average in the Easy tab — MC doesn't care that this is "RL"; it's still just "average the returns you observed." The only RL-specific part is that the returns Gt come from episodes (trajectories), not single die rolls.

Complex: Full DP Sweep vs. One MC Episode on a 3-State Chain

Consider a tiny 3-state chain: s1 → s2 → s3 (terminal). From s1, action "right" always moves to s2 with reward −1. From s2, action "right" always moves to s3 (terminal) with reward +10. Assume γ = 1.

  1. What a full DP sweep uses: DP needs p(s2,−1|s1,right) = 1 and p(s3,+10|s2,right) = 1 — the complete model for both transitions, known in advance, before any interaction happens.
  2. DP computes, in one backward sweep (bootstrapping): V(s2) = −1 + γ·V(s3) = −1 + 0 = −1 (s3 terminal, V(s3)=0), then V(s1) = −1 + γ·V(s2) = −1 + (−1) = −2. Exact — no sampling, no episodes run.
  3. What one MC episode uses: the agent starts at s1, takes "right" (reward −1, → s2), takes "right" again (reward +10, → s3, terminal). That's the entire trajectory τ = (s1, right, −1, s2, right, +10, s3).
  4. Wrong path (common mistake): a student might compute V(s1) using only the immediate reward, V(s1) "=" −1, treating it like a one-step DP backup. This is wrong — MC does not bootstrap, so V(s1) must use the full return from s1 to the terminal state, not just the first reward.
  5. Correct MC computation: processing backward from the terminal state: G(s2) = +10 (reward after s2's first visit, to terminal) → Returns(s2) gets +10. G(s1) = −1 + γ·G(s2) = −1 + 10 = +9 → Returns(s1) gets +9. After just this one episode: V(s1) ≈ 9, V(s2) ≈ 10.
  6. Compare what each method "used": DP used the transition probabilities for both steps, computed exactly, with zero interaction. MC used zero probabilities — only the one trajectory that actually happened — but its estimate (V(s1)≈9 from one episode) will have sampling variance if rewards were stochastic; here, with deterministic transitions, MC's single-episode estimate (V(s1)=9, V(s2)=10) actually disagrees with DP's exact answer (V(s1)=−2, V(s2)=−1) — go back and check: did DP's sweep use the same reward signs as the episode?
  7. Resolution: Re-reading the setup — DP computed V(s1) = −1 + γV(s2) where V(s2) = −1, giving −2. But the MC episode's return from s1 is G(s1) = R(s1→s2) + γ·R(s2→s3) = −1 + 10 = +9. These differ because DP's V(s2) = −1 only reflects the reward leaving s2 (−1... but s2's actual exit reward in this chain is +10!). This discrepancy is the point: always double-check which reward is associated with which transition before comparing DP and MC numbers — a single sign or reward-assignment error changes the whole trace. With rewards as stated (s1→s2 = −1, s2→s3 = +10), the consistent DP computation is V(s2) = +10 + γ·V(s3) = +10, V(s1) = −1 + γ·V(s2) = −1 + 10 = +9 — which now matches MC's single-episode estimate exactly, because the chain is deterministic (zero variance).
Answer: V(s1) = +9, V(s2) = +10 (both DP, exact, and MC, this episode, agree here because the chain is deterministic — MC would still show variance across multiple episodes if rewards or transitions were stochastic). DP needed the full model p(s',r|s,a) for both transitions, computed with zero interaction. MC needed zero model knowledge, but had to run the episode to completion before computing any value.
Key Insight: on a deterministic chain, DP and MC converge to the same numbers — the difference is entirely in what information each method consumed to get there (full model vs. one full trajectory) and when the value becomes available (immediately for DP vs. only after the episode ends for MC).

Part C — Exam Alert

Exam Alert 🔥 endsem hot

Closed-Book Form: "What Does Model-Free Mean?"

Expect a short-answer prompt like: "Explain the difference between model-based and model-free reinforcement learning." Closed-book answer:

Model-based (DP): the agent has access to p(s',r|s,a) — the probability of every next-state/reward outcome for every state-action pair — and can compute vπ directly via the Bellman expectation equation, using full backups and bootstrapping. Model-free (MC): the agent has no access to p(s',r|s,a); it estimates vπ(s) and qπ(s,a) by generating episodes, observing actual returns Gt, and averaging them — no bootstrapping, sample backups only.

Common Mistake: "Model-Free" ≠ "Policy-Free"

A very common error is to think model-free RL has no policy at all — as if the agent is "just wandering randomly with no structure." This is wrong. MC methods absolutely have a policy π(a|s) — in fact the whole PE-PI cycle (Section A5 above) is about evaluating and improving that policy. What MC lacks is the transition/reward model p(s',r|s,a), not the policy. The policy starts arbitrary/random and is refined; it is never absent.

Section 2: Monte Carlo Prediction — First-Visit & Every-Visit Policy Evaluation

📌 Recall from Section 1: DP needs the model p(s′,r|s,a); MC throws that requirement away and learns from episodes instead.
📌 The DP-vs-MC comparison table (Section 1) is your master reference — DP bootstraps (one-step lookahead, BFS-like); MC must finish the whole episode (depth-first).
📌 The sampling idea from Section 1: MC = "good sampling-based estimation" — like estimating average height of 10,000 students by measuring a sample, not everyone.

Bridge: where DP computed V(s) by sweeping the full model, MC computes V(s) by averaging the actual returns observed across many episodes — same target value, completely different source of information (full table in Section 1).

The Problem

We know vπ(s) should be the expected return from state s — but we don't have the model to compute that expectation exactly (no p(s′,r|s,a), see Section 1). MC's answer is almost embarrassingly simple: just run episodes, collect the actual returns, and average them. No transition probabilities. No Bellman expectation. Just arithmetic on what actually happened.

INPUT → policy π to be evaluated, and a set of episodes generated by following π
PROCESS → for each episode: for each first visit to state s, compute the return Gt from that point to the end of the episode, and append it to Returns(s)
OUTPUT → V(s) ≈ average(Returns(s)) for every state visited

The Formulas

Gt = Rt+1 + γRt+2 + γ2Rt+3 + ...

In English: G_t is the return from time t — the sum of every (discounted) future reward you actually receive, from this point until the episode ends. R = reward at a given time step; γ = discount factor.

First-visit MC: V(s) ≈ average of Gt over all FIRST visits to s across episodes

In English: to estimate how good state s is, look at every episode that visited s at least once, take the return starting from the first time s appeared in that episode, and average those returns across all such episodes. This is the examinable form — first-visit is the syllabus's primary MC prediction algorithm.

Every-visit MC: V(s) ≈ average of Gt over ALL visits to s (within and across episodes)

In English: instead of stopping at the first occurrence, count every time s appears — each occurrence contributes its own return to the average. Toy numerical (transcript): for a single episode where state S occurs twice, the first occurrence gives G = 2; first-visit MC stops there, so V(S) = 2. Every-visit MC also keeps the second occurrence's return, G = 3, and averages both: V(S) = (2 + 3) / 2 = 2.5. Same episode, two different numbers — that's the counting difference.

Slide 8 — annotated: "First-Visit MC Policy Evaluation [estimate Vπ(s)]" — Initialize Returns(s) as empty for all s. Repeat forever: generate an episode using π; for each state s appearing in the episode, let R be the return following the first occurrence of s, append R to Returns(s), and set V(s) ← average(Returns(s)).

You just visit once, come out, and gather your experience from that visit." (→ first-visit) "But if you are a person living there — a delivery partner living in that city — you might have to gather knowledge from every of your experiences." (→ every-visit). Used for delivery routing, path planning, traffic, stock trading, patient diagnosis.
Prof. Sangeetha — cs4_1 Transcript §1:11:51 (TV / power-outage analogy — why episodes restart): "Say, suddenly power goes in your house... when the power comes, will the TV start from the news channel or will the set-top box restart again?" — it restarts from scratch. A robot that hits a wall in an episode doesn't "walk back" to the start; the interaction simply restarts as a fresh episode. This is why each episode is treated as an independent sample.
Why this matters: First-visit MC gives you exactly one sample per episode for state s — and because each episode is an independent run (TV-restart analogy above), these samples are i.i.d. (independent and identically distributed). That's what gives first-visit MC its clean convergence theory. Every-visit MC, by contrast, can pull multiple correlated returns from the same episode (they share trailing reward sequences) — useful for data efficiency, but messier to prove convergence for. See the Research Insight below.
S&B Canonical Why averaging works — the Law of Large Numbers argument: By definition vπ(s) = Eπ[Gt | St=s] — an expectation. First-visit MC collects one return Gi per episode that visits s, and each Gi is drawn from the same underlying return distribution (same policy, same MDP, same starting state s) — and episodes are independent runs, so the Gi's are i.i.d. The LLN then guarantees: (1/M) Σ Gi → vπ(s) as M → ∞. The estimate is unbiased for every finite M, and the standard error shrinks as σ/√M — more episodes ⇒ tighter estimate, but at a slow 1/√M rate (4× the episodes to halve the error). (Source: Sutton & Barto 2nd ed, Ch.5 — Topic A.)

Answer: Just one — the return G2 computed from step 2 (the first occurrence). The return from step 5 (G5) is not added to Returns(s) for this episode — that would be every-visit (or worse, double-counting). See the Complex tab below for the full trace.

  • DO: Compute V(s) by averaging first-visit returns across episodes — just identify the first occurrence of s per episode, sum the rewards from there to the end (with discounting), and average.
  • EXPLAIN: Why first-visit returns are i.i.d. (independent episodes, one sample per episode) and why that gives MC clean LLN-based convergence — and why every-visit trades that cleanliness for more data per episode.
  • WATCH FOR: Double-counting a state's second visit within the same episode as if it were a separate first-visit sample — and getting the direction of Gt backwards (it's the sum of future rewards from t onward, never past rewards).

Easy: V(s) = 0.5 from four episode returns

Setup (Slide 9): across 6 episodes, state s appears in only 4 of them. The first-visit return recorded in each of those 4 episodes is: +2, +1, -5, +4.

Complex: a state visited TWICE in one episode (steps 2 and 5)

Suppose a single episode visits state s at time step 2 and again at time step 5 before terminating. Using the toy numerical from the transcript (γ=1): the rewards following step 2 are +1, −2, 0, +1, −3, +5, giving G2 = 1 + (−2) + 0 + 1 + (−3) + 5 = 2. ❌ The WRONG calculation (double-counting)
  1. Treat both occurrences as separate "first-visit" samples for this episode.
  2. Returns(s) gets two entries from this one episode: [2, 3].

✅ The CORRECT first-visit calculation

  3. Append only 2 to Returns(s) for this episode. The step-5 occurrence contributes nothing extra.
  4. Returns(s) for this episode = [2] — exactly one sample, as first-visit MC requires.
V(s) contribution from this episode = 2 (one sample, not two)

Section 3: From State Values to Action Values — MC Estimation of Q(s,a) and Generalized Policy Iteration

Just as DP's policy iteration alternates between evaluating vπ and improving π by acting greedily on it, MC control will alternate between estimating Q(s,a) from episodes and improving π greedily on those estimates — same loop, no model required.

Why V(s) Alone Cannot Drive Control

Section 2 gave you a way to estimate V(s) for every state by averaging first-visit returns. Suppose you now have a perfect V(s) for every state in the A–E gridworld. Can you act greedily — i.e. always pick the best action?

To act greedily on V(s), you would need to ask: "which action leads to the s′ with the highest V(s′)?" But answering "which action leads to which s′" is exactly the transition model p(s′,r|s,a) — the thing MC threw away in Section 1! Without it, V(s) tells you how good a state is, but not which action got you there or where any action will take you next.

Q(s,a) sidesteps this completely — it is indexed directly by the action. To act greedily, you simply compute argmaxa Q(s,a) and take that action. No model, no "which s′ does this lead to" lookup. This is the single idea that turns MC from an evaluation tool into a control tool.

The Local-Optimum Trap

Once Q(w,E) = −1 looks worse than Q(w,W) = +1, the greedy policy never selects action E from w again. But "never selected" means "never updated" — its first-visit return list Returns(w,E) stops growing forever. If the true long-run value of going E from w (eventually reaching the +10 treasure at y) is actually higher than going W, a purely greedy policy can never discover this — the (s,a) pair that would reveal it is permanently frozen out.

This is exactly the gap GPI's "improve" step doesn't close on its own: improvement is only as good as the Q-estimates it acts on, and greedy action selection stops collecting new data for the very pairs whose estimates might be wrong. Why This Matters — This Is the Moment MC Becomes Control

  • Evaluation → Control: Section 2 only ever told you "how good is this state under the current policy." With Q(s,a) and the GPI loop above, MC can now change the policy — the defining feature of a control method.
  • Direct parallel to policy iteration: The evaluate→improve→repeat structure is identical to Sessions 3-4's policy iteration. The only thing that changed is how evaluation happens (sampling instead of solving equations) and what we evaluate (Q instead of V, so improvement needs no model).
  • But a new problem appears: greedy improvement on noisy, partially-explored Q-estimates can lock in a local optimum — the {u,w,x,y,z} example. This sets up exactly the problem Section 4's ε-soft / GLIE machinery exists to solve.
S&B Canonical

MC Enables Control Without a Model

The reason MC can replace DP's policy iteration entirely model-free comes down to where the "improve" step gets its information. DP's policy improvement theorem compares qπ(s,a) values that are computed from the model via a one-step lookahead: qπ(s,a) = Σs′,r p(s′,r|s,a)[r + γvπ(s′)]. MC instead estimates the same qπ(s,a) directly, as the expected return Eπ[Gt | St=s, At=a], by sampling complete episodes and averaging — no Σs′,r p(s′,r|s,a) term anywhere.

Because the policy-improvement theorem only requires that qπ(s,a) ≥ vπ(s) for the greedy action to guarantee improvement — and says nothing about how qπ was obtained — an MC-estimated Q(s,a) can be substituted directly into the same theorem. This is the formal reason "MC control" is a legitimate control method, not just an approximation: (Standard convergence theory, Sutton & Barto 2nd ed, Ch.5)

Self-Check: Why can't we act greedily using only V(s), without a model — what extra information does Q(s,a) give us directly that V(s) does not?
V(s) tells you how good being in a state is, but not which action leads where — to find the best action you'd need to know, for each action, which next state s′ it leads to (and with what probability/reward), which is precisely the transition model p(s′,r|s,a). Q(s,a) is indexed directly by the action, so argmaxa Q(s,a) immediately tells you the best action to take from s — no model, no "which s′ does this lead to" step required.

Section 4: On-Policy MC Control for ε-Soft Policies — Solving the Exploration Problem 🔥 endsem hot

A1. Picking Up the Cliffhanger

At the end of Section 3, we ran the full GPI loop on the {u,w,x,y,z} grid: generate an episode, update Q(s,a) by first-visit averaging, then improve π by going greedy — always pick argmaxa Q(s,a). The result? The policy converged to u → exit, w → W, x → E, y → exit, z → exit — and got stuck there. The path through y, which carries a reward of +10, was never explored after the early episodes, so its Q-value never improved. The agent locked onto a locally-good but globally suboptimal policy and had no way out.

Why This Matters

This isn't a bug in the averaging — the averaging is correct for the data it sees. The bug is in what data it sees. A purely greedy policy never tries an action it currently ranks low, so that action's Q-value is frozen forever at whatever (possibly noisy, possibly wrong) estimate it had early on. ε-soft policies fix this by making sure every action always retains a nonzero chance of being tried — nothing is ever permanently ruled out.

One-sentence DP-vs-MC bridge: DP never needed exploration because policy evaluation swept over the entire state-action space using the known model p(s',r|s,a); MC only learns about what it actually samples, so how it samples — i.e. the exploration built into the policy itself — becomes part of the algorithm, not an afterthought.

A2. The Fix: ε-Soft Policies

An ε-soft policy is any policy that never assigns probability zero to any action:

π(a|s) ≥ ε / |A(s)| for all a ∈ A(s)

In English: for every state s, and every possible action a, the policy must give that action some chance — at least ε divided by the number of available actions. So nothing is ever permanently ruled out; every action always has SOME chance.

The most common ε-soft policy is ε-greedy:

π(a|s) = { 1 − ε + ε/|A(s)| if a = A*
ε/|A(s)| if a ≠ A*

In English: with probability 1−ε you pick the greedy action A* = argmaxa Q(s,a) outright. But on top of that, the remaining ε probability is split uniformly across all actions (greedy action included) — that's why the greedy action's total probability is 1 − ε + ε/|A(s)|, not just 1−ε. Every non-greedy action gets exactly ε/|A(s)|.

Prof Annotation — ε as a Hyperparameter

"Epsilon is a parameter... a hyperparameter which talks about how much of exploration do you have to allow in this scenario. You start with an arbitrary policy... but at times, for a certain — with a certain probability, randomly you choose actions [instead of] whatsoever the actions the policy suggests... but the remaining one minus epsilon time, you strictly only exploit."

"I start with an arbitrary policy and I'm updating the same ε-soft policy. So there is only one policy that I'm talking about and I keep on updating it." — explaining why this is called on-policy: the same ε-soft policy both generates episodes AND gets improved, like learning to drive a car by driving it yourself and adjusting as you go.

A3. GLIE — Letting the Policy Eventually Become Truly Optimal

A fixed ε > 0 guarantees exploration forever — but it also means the policy never becomes purely optimal; it always retains some probability of acting randomly, even after Q has converged. The fix is to let ε shrink over time according to GLIE (Greedy in the Limit with Infinite Exploration):

εt → 0 as t → ∞ (e.g. εt = 1/t)

In English: shrink the exploration rate toward zero as training progresses, but slowly enough (like 1/t) that every state-action pair still gets sampled an unbounded number of times along the way ("infinite exploration"), while the policy itself becomes greedy ("greedy in the limit") once the Q-values have converged.

Why this matters: GLIE gives the best of both worlds — enough exploration early on that no (s,a) pair is ever structurally excluded, but a final policy that is asymptotically π* (the truly optimal policy), not a permanently-slightly-random one.

A4. The Algorithm: On-Policy First-Visit ε-Soft MC Control

Input Algorithm parameter: small ε > 0 Initialize π ← an arbitrary ε-soft policy Q(s,a) ∈ ℝ (arbitrarily), for all s ∈ S, a ∈ A(s) Returns(s,a) ← empty list, for all s ∈ S, a ∈ A(s) Process — repeat forever (for each episode) Generate an episode following π: S₀, A₀, R₁, ..., S_(T-1), A_(T-1), R_T G ← 0 Loop for each step of episode, t = T-1, T-2, ..., 0: G ← γG + R_(t+1) Unless the pair (S_t, A_t) appears earlier in (S₀,A₀), ..., (S_(t-1),A_(t-1)): ← first-visit check Append G to Returns(S_t, A_t) Q(S_t, A_t) ← average(Returns(S_t, A_t)) A* ← argmax_a Q(S_t, a) (ties broken arbitrarily) For all a ∈ A(S_t): π(a|S_t) ← 1 - ε + ε/|A(S_t)| if a = A* π(a|S_t) ← ε/|A(S_t)| if a ≠ A* Output π converges toward an ε-greedy-optimal policy (asymptotically π* under a GLIE ε schedule)
Prof Annotation — Slide 15

"The ε-soft policy ensures all actions are tried with at least probability ε/|A(s)|, avoiding getting stuck at local optima." Notice: the episode is processed backward (t = T−1 down to 0) so that G ← γG + Rt+1 accumulates the return incrementally, and the policy-improvement step happens immediately after each Q-update within the same episode — not at the end. This is GPI-style interleaving of evaluation and improvement, applied to (s,a) pairs instead of states.

Recapping the steps verbally: "...find out the argmax action, assign it as A* in that particular state... then update the action probabilities for greedy and non-greedy actions, [restart] for it, and start again." And on scope: "for our exam point of view, we are going to go with only first visit policy evaluation and control... control algorithm would be only with first visit."

Why This Matters

This is the first exploration strategy you've seen that is baked directly into the policy itself, rather than being a property of the environment or the algorithm's sweep order. DP never needed this — with the full model p(s',r|s,a), policy evaluation considers every state-action pair on every sweep, so "exploration" was never a question. MC only ever learns from what it samples — so the sampling policy itself must guarantee coverage. ε-soft is that guarantee.

Research Enhanced

ε-Soft Is the Practical Substitute for Exploring Starts

MC control's convergence proof needs every (s,a) pair to be visited infinitely often. There are two ways to guarantee this. Exploring Starts (ES) — covered briefly last session — satisfies it by starting every episode from a randomly chosen (s,a) pair, with a fully greedy policy thereafter. This is clean in simulators (you can reset to any state and force any first action) but impractical in the real world (you cannot teleport a robot or a live system into an arbitrary configuration). ε-soft policies satisfy the same requirement differently: instead of randomizing how episodes start, they randomize action selection at every step, guaranteeing every (s,a) pair has probability ≥ ε/|A(s)| > 0 of being chosen whenever its state is visited. Over infinitely many episodes, an event with positive probability that recurs at every opportunity occurs infinitely often — so no action is ever permanently locked out, and every Q(s,a) keeps receiving fresh samples. ε-greedy isn't a hack bolted onto MC control; it's the mechanism that fulfils, in any environment, the same theoretical requirement that Exploring Starts fulfils only in resettable simulators.

Source: Standard convergence theory (Sutton & Barto 2nd ed, Ch.5)

    x --(←, r=0)--> w --(exit, r=-100)--> TERMINAL
  2. Step 2 — Compute G backward (γ = 0.9):
    G(w, exit) = 0.9 × 0 + (−100) = −100
    G(x, ←) = 0.9 × (−100) + 0 = −90

    Returns(x,←) = [−90, −81]
    Q(x,←) = average([−90, −81]) = (−90 + −81) / 2 = −85.5

Runnable Implementation — On-Policy First-Visit ε-Greedy MC Control

Below is a minimal, correct NumPy implementation on a tiny 1D corridor gridworld that mirrors the {w,x,y,z} structure used above (poison on the left, treasure on the right). It implements the pipeline box exactly: generate episode under ε-greedy π → process backward, compute G → first-visit update of Q via running average → ε-greedy policy improvement. 

import numpy as np

# ----- Tiny corridor environment: states 0..4, state 0 = "poison" exit (-100),
#       state 4 = "treasure" exit (+10). Actions: 0 = left, 1 = right, 2 = exit.
N_STATES = 5
ACTIONS = [0, 1, 2]          # 0=left, 1=right, 2=exit
GAMMA = 0.9
EPSILON = 0.1
N_EPISODES = 2000
MAX_STEPS = 20

def step(s, a):
    """Returns (reward, next_state, done)."""
    if a == 2:  # exit
        if s == 0:
            return -100.0, s, True
        if s == N_STATES - 1:
            return 10.0, s, True
        return -1.0, s, True       # exiting elsewhere: small penalty
    if a == 0:  # left
        return 0.0, max(s - 1, 0), False
    # a == 1: right
    return 0.0, min(s + 1, N_STATES - 1), False

def epsilon_greedy_action(Q, s, eps):
    if np.random.rand() < eps:
        return np.random.choice(ACTIONS)
    return int(np.argmax(Q[s]))

def generate_episode(Q, eps, start_state=2):
    """Returns list of (s, a, r) tuples, S0..S_{T-1},A_{T-1},R_T."""
    episode = []
    s = start_state
    for _ in range(MAX_STEPS):
        a = epsilon_greedy_action(Q, s, eps)
        r, s_next, done = step(s, a)
        episode.append((s, a, r))
        if done:
            break
        s = s_next
    return episode

def mc_control(n_episodes=N_EPISODES, eps=EPSILON, gamma=GAMMA):
    Q = np.zeros((N_STATES, len(ACTIONS)))
    returns_sum = {}    # (s,a) -> running sum of returns
    returns_cnt = {}    # (s,a) -> count of returns

    for _ in range(n_episodes):
        episode = generate_episode(Q, eps)
        G = 0.0
        visited = set()
        # process backward: t = T-1, T-2, ..., 0
        for t in range(len(episode) - 1, -1, -1):
            s, a, r = episode[t]
            G = gamma * G + r          # G <- gamma*G + R_{t+1}
            if (s, a) not in visited:  # first-visit check
                visited.add((s, a))
                returns_sum[(s, a)] = returns_sum.get((s, a), 0.0) + G
                returns_cnt[(s, a)] = returns_cnt.get((s, a), 0) + 1
                Q[s, a] = returns_sum[(s, a)] / returns_cnt[(s, a)]
                # policy improvement happens implicitly: epsilon_greedy_action
                # always reads the latest Q, so A* = argmax_a Q(s,a) is used
                # next time s is visited -- matching pi(a|s) <- 1-eps+eps/|A|
                # for a=A*, eps/|A| otherwise.
    return Q

if __name__ == "__main__":
    Q = mc_control()
    print("Final Q-table (rows=states, cols=[left, right, exit]):")
    print(np.round(Q, 2))
    greedy_actions = np.argmax(Q, axis=1)
    names = {0: "left", 1: "right", 2: "exit"}
    print("Greedy policy:", [names[a] for a in greedy_actions])

Running this converges to a policy that moves right from the middle states toward the +10 treasure exit and avoids the −100 poison exit on the left — the same qualitative outcome as the {w,x,y,z} example above, recovered automatically because ε keeps every action sampling forever.

Closed-Book Formula Form — Know Cold

ε-greedy policy:

π(a|s) = 1 − ε + ε/|A(s)| if a = A*      ε/|A(s)| if a ≠ A*

Backward return accumulation (process episode T−1 → 0):

G ← γG + Rt+1

Algorithm skeleton (write from memory):

  1. Init small ε, arbitrary ε-soft π, Q(s,a), empty Returns(s,a) for all s,a
  2. Repeat: generate episode under π; set G=0
  3. For t = T−1 down to 0: G ← γG + Rt+1
  4. If (S_t,A_t) is a first visit: append G to Returns(S_t,A_t); Q(S_t,A_t) ← average(Returns)
  5. A* ← argmax_a Q(S_t,a); set π(A*|S_t) = 1−ε+ε/|A(S_t)|, all other π(a|S_t) = ε/|A(S_t)|

Common Mistakes (and Why They're Wrong)

  • Mistake 1 — Greedy action gets probability "1−ε": Wrong. The greedy action gets 1−ε+ε/|A(s)| — it ALSO receives its share of the ε pot that's distributed uniformly over all actions. Forgetting the "+ε/|A(s)|" term is the single most common error on this topic.
  • Mistake 2 — Computing Gt forward instead of backward: Wrong. The algorithm explicitly processes the episode from t=T−1 down to t=0, with G ← γG + Rt+1 starting from G=0 at the terminal. Computing forward gives incorrect discount exponents and an incorrect first-visit order.
  • Mistake 3 — Forgetting the first-visit check applies to (s,a) pairs: For MC control, "first visit" means the first occurrence of the state-action pair (St,At) in the episode — not just the state.

The Gap: One Policy Generates the Data, Another Wants to Learn

Sections 2–4 were all on-policy: you generate episodes by following policy π, and you use those same episodes to evaluate or improve that same π. The moment π changes, your old episodes become stale — every new policy needs fresh data generated under itself. That's why on-policy MC is not sample-efficient.

What if you want to evaluate or improve a different, target policy π, while only having episodes generated by some other, behavior policy b? That's off-policy learning: π is "something we are going to optimize," b is "more like an all-action-allowed policy" used to generate experience (Prof. Sangeetha — cs4_2 Transcript §Off-Policy Introduction [Beyond Mid-Sem]). For this to even be possible, every action π might take must also be reachable under b — this is the coverage condition: π(a|s) > 0 ⇒ b(a|s) > 0 (Slide 29 — [APPENDIX-ONLY]).

The Importance-Sampling Ratio ρt

The fix is to re-weight each observed return by how much more (or less) likely the trajectory you actually saw is under π compared to under b. That re-weighting factor is the importance-sampling ratio ρt:

ρt = ∏k=tT−1 π(Ak|Sk) / b(Ak|Sk)

In English: ρt = how much more or less likely the observed trajectory is under π compared to under b = the product of the π(a|s)/b(a|s) ratios for every action taken from time t to the end of the episode. The unknown environment transition probabilities cancel out of this ratio entirely — "no model needed" (Slide 30 — [APPENDIX-ONLY]).

Prof. Sangeetha — cs4_2 Transcript §Importance Sampling Ratio Definition [Beyond Mid-Sem]: "Importance sampling ratio is nothing but the ratio of the probability of occurrence of a state-action pair in the target policy to its probability in the behavioural policy" — aggregated over the whole trajectory.

One Worked Example: Grid W → X → Y

Same robot-grid flavour as Sections 2–4, now with two policies. Behavior policy b = {right: 0.5, left: 0.5}; target policy π = {right: 0.99, left: 0.01}. Episode τ1 = W →(right)→ X →(right)→ Y → exit, return G = 10 (Slide 32 / Transcript2 §Grid Scenario — [APPENDIX-ONLY]).

Ordinary IS — high variance

ρ(τ1) = (0.99/0.5) × (0.99/0.5) = 3.96  →  Vπ(W) = 3.96 × 10 = 39.6  (M=1)

After a 2nd episode τ2 (W →(left)→ exit, G=1, ρ=0.02): Vπ(W) = (3.96×10 + 0.02×1) / 2 ≈ 19.8 (M=2)

Why high variance: ordinary IS divides by the episode count M, so a single trajectory with a huge ratio (0.99/0.5 compounded twice = 3.96) can swing the estimate to 39.6 in one episode and down to 19.8 the next — the ratio itself can get arbitrarily large, dragging the average all over the place even though the estimate is unbiased.

Weighted IS — lower variance

Vπ(W) = [Σ ρmGm] / [Σ ρm] = (3.96×10 + 0.02×1) / (3.96 + 0.02) = 39.62 / 3.98 ≈ 3.98  (after τ1, Vπ(W) = 10; after τ2, ≈ 9.5–10)

Why lower variance: weighted IS normalizes by the sum of the importance ratios instead of the raw episode count M — so an unusually large ρ inflates both the numerator and the denominator together, keeping the estimate bounded and consistent across episodes ("there's no variance here, right?" — Prof. Sangeetha, cs4_2 Transcript §Weighted Importance Sampling [Beyond Mid-Sem]). Trade-off: weighted IS is biased while M is small, but that bias shrinks as more episodes arrive, and in practice it is "almost always preferred" (Slide 33 — [APPENDIX-ONLY]).

Where MC Goes Next: Bridging to TD

Every MC method in this guide — on-policy or off-policy — shares one constraint: it waits until an episode ends before it updates anything, because that's the only point at which the true return Gt is known. But what if episodes are very long, or in continuing tasks don't end at all? Temporal-Difference (TD) methods (Sessions 9–10) remove that constraint: they update the value estimate after every single step, using a bootstrapped guess of the return instead of waiting for the real one — blending MC's idea of learning from sampled experience with DP's idea of bootstrapping off current estimates. As Sutton puts it, TD is "the best of both worlds."

Conceptual Deep Dives

These four boxes step back from the worked examples and look at the why behind Monte Carlo methods — useful for building real understanding, and for the kind of "explain why" questions that show up alongside the numericals.

Deep Dive A

The DP → MC Transition: What Actually Changes

Sections 1-4 made one conceptual jump over and over: replace "sum over everything that could happen, weighted by its probability" (DP) with "run it once, see what actually happened, average over many runs" (MC). The two backup diagrams below make this concrete.

DP Backup — Shallow & Wide

        s
       /|\\
      a1 a2 a3      ← every action
     /|\\ /|\\ /|\\
    s' s' s' ...    ← every successor,
                       weighted by p(s',r|s,a)
                       using CURRENT V(s')

One step deep, but branches across every action and every possible outcome. Needs the full model p(s',r|s,a). Uses bootstrapped V(s') at the leaves — this is what "bootstrapping" means.

MC Backup — Deep & Narrow

        s
        |
        a    ← the action actually taken
        |
        s'
        |
        a
        |
        s''
        |
       ...
        |
      TERMINAL  ← G_t = actual return,
                    no model, no V(s') used

One single path, as deep as the episode itself, with no branching — only the one sequence of states/actions/rewards that actually occurred. The backup uses the real accumulated return Gt, never an estimate of a successor's value.

This single picture explains almost every difference between DP and MC:

  • Model-free vs model-based — the DP diagram literally requires p(s',r|s,a) to know which branches exist and how likely each is. The MC diagram needs nothing of the kind: it just follows whatever the environment (or simulator) actually produces.
  • Bootstrapping vs no bootstrapping — DP's leaves are current estimates V(s'), which may themselves be wrong early on (bias from not-yet-converged estimates). MC's "leaf" is the terminal state itself — Gt is the real, fully-realized sum of rewards, so there is nothing left to estimate.
  • Exact-but-model-dependent vs unbiased-but-high-variance — given a correct model, DP's full backup is computed exactly (no sampling noise at all); its only error is from not having iterated enough. MC's sample backup is an honest, unbiased sample of Gt, but any single episode can swing wildly depending on which random path was taken — hence high variance (quantified in Deep Dive B).
Research Enhanced

Put another way: DP asks "if I considered every possibility right now, weighted correctly, what would I conclude?" — an exhaustive, instantaneous calculation. MC asks "what actually happened, and what happens on average if I let it happen many times?" — an empirical, experiential calculation. Every other DP-vs-MC difference (Section 1's comparison table) is a consequence of this one reframing.

Deep Dive B

Why MC Converges: The Law of Large Numbers Argument

Section 2 asserted that first-visit MC's running average V(s) converges to vπ(s). Here's why that's guaranteed, not just plausible.

vπ(s) = Eπ[ Gt | St = s ]

vπ(s) is defined as an expectation — the mean of the return's probability distribution, given that you're in state s and follow π forever after. First-visit MC's whole strategy is: estimate this expectation by averaging samples drawn from that exact distribution.

Each time state s is visited for the first time in an episode, the return Gi that follows is one sample from the distribution of "returns given St=s, following π." Two properties make these samples especially well-behaved:

  • Identically distributed — same policy π, same MDP, same starting state s, every time. The distribution doesn't shift between episodes.
  • Independent — different episodes are independent runs (no shared randomness), and taking only the first visit per episode means there's exactly one sample per episode — no within-episode correlation to worry about.

Samples that are i.i.d. with mean vπ(s) are exactly the setting the Law of Large Numbers (LLN) is built for:

V(s) = (1/M) Σ Gi  →  vπ(s)   as M → ∞

The sample mean of M i.i.d. draws converges to the true expectation as M grows — this is the same principle behind estimating π by throwing darts at a square, or estimating an integral by random sampling. First-visit MC is "Monte Carlo estimation of an expectation," in the most literal sense.

For every finite M, the estimator is also unbiased: E[V(s)] = vπ(s) exactly — there's no systematic skew, only sampling noise.

How fast? The 1/√M rate

SE = σ / √M

σ² = Varπ[Gt | St=s] is the variance of the return distribution. The standard error of the average shrinks as 1/√M — so 4× the episodes are needed to halve the error. Long, stochastic episodes (large σ) need proportionally more episodes for the same precision. This is the fundamental "price" of being model-free: DP can become arbitrarily accurate with more sweeps of the same model at no extra "data cost"; MC's accuracy is capped by how many episodes you can afford to run.

What changes for every-visit MC?

Every-visit MC also converges to vπ(s), but the clean story above doesn't apply directly:

  • If s is visited more than once in an episode, those visits' returns share the same trailing trajectory — they are correlated, not independent.
  • Because of that correlation, every-visit MC is technically biased for finite M (though the bias is small and vanishes as M→∞ — asymptotically unbiased).
  • In practice every-visit MC is often more data-efficient (more samples per episode), but its convergence proof needs more care (ergodicity/martingale-style arguments) than the textbook i.i.d. LLN that first-visit MC enjoys directly.
Research Enhanced

Takeaway: first-visit MC is the "textbook clean" estimator — i.i.d. samples, LLN, unbiased, with a known 1/√M convergence rate. Every-visit MC trades that clean guarantee for better use of the data in each episode. Both converge; they just get there by different routes.

Deep Dive C

Exploration: The Full Picture

Section 3 showed the {u,w,x,y,z} trap; Section 4 showed the ε-soft fix. Here's how the pieces connect into one coherent theory — and why DP never needed any of this.

Step 1 — Why greedy MC control gets stuck

MC control improves its policy from Q(s,a) estimates built only from returns the agent actually experienced. A purely greedy policy — always argmaxaQ(s,a) — creates a feedback loop:

  • The greedy action keeps getting chosen → its Q-estimate keeps refining.
  • Every other action is never chosen again → its Q-estimate is frozen at whatever value it had — possibly from a single early, unlucky, or just-initialized episode.
  • A frozen, unvisited action can never be proven better — even if it truly is.

This is exactly Section 3's {u,w,x,y,z} story: once Q(w, exit-left) looked better than the untried path toward y's +10 treasure, the greedy policy locked in and never looked back. The trap isn't a "wrong landscape" — there is no search over a landscape. It's that some (s,a) pairs are structurally excluded from ever being sampled again.

Step 2 — The ε-soft fix

Section 4's fix: require π(a|s) ≥ ε/|A(s)| for every action in every state — i.e. no action probability ever collapses to exactly 0. The concrete instance is ε-greedy: probability (1−ε) on the greedy action, probability ε spread uniformly over all actions.

Why this fixes the trap: as long as ε > 0, every (s,a) pair retains a probability bounded away from zero on every visit to s. Over infinitely many episodes, that means every (s,a) pair is selected infinitely often — by a law-of-large-numbers / Borel-Cantelli style argument, an event with positive probability that recurs at every opportunity must occur infinitely often. So:

  • Every Q(s,a) keeps receiving new samples and keeps improving.
  • No action is ever permanently locked out.
  • MC control's convergence requirement — every state-action pair visited infinitely often — is satisfied.

Step 3 — GLIE: having it both ways

An ε-soft policy that stays soft forever never becomes truly optimal — it always retains some chance of acting randomly. GLIE (Greedy in the Limit with Infinite Exploration) resolves this with a decaying schedule:

  • Infinite exploration: εt > 0 for every t, so every (s,a) pair can still be sampled at every step.
  • Greedy in the limit: εt → 0 as t → ∞, so the policy's behavior converges to greedy w.r.t. the now-converged Q.

A classic schedule is εt = 1/t — it decays slowly enough that cumulative exploration is still "infinite" (Σεt diverges), yet εt→0 so the asymptotic policy is the optimal deterministic one.

Step 4 — The road not taken: Exploring Starts

There's a second, older way to guarantee infinite visitation: Exploring Starts (ES). Every episode begins from a randomly chosen (s0,a0) pair — every pair must have nonzero probability of being a starting pair — and the policy itself can stay fully deterministic/greedy throughout.

AspectExploring Starts (ES)ε-soft (e.g. ε-greedy)
Policy during learningFully deterministic / greedyStochastic until ε→0
Exploration comes fromRandomized episode initializationRandomness in every action choice
PracticalityOnly feasible with full simulator reset controlWorks in any environment, no special reset
GuaranteeEvery (s,a) sampled ∞ often via start-state distributionEvery (s,a) sampled ∞ often via per-step probabilities ≥ ε/|A(s)|

ES requires the ability to teleport the environment to an arbitrary state and force an arbitrary first action — trivial in a gridworld simulator, essentially impossible for a real robot, a live recommendation system, or any physical process. This is exactly the gap ε-soft / on-policy methods fill: they put the burden of exploration on the policy's action-selection rule rather than on the environment's reset mechanism.

Step 5 — Why DP never had this problem

DP's policy improvement step recomputes V(s) or Q(s,a) for every state-action pair on every sweep, using the model p(s',r|s,a) — there is no "unvisited" pair, because nothing is visited at all in the experiential sense. Every pair's value is recomputed from the model every iteration, regardless of what the current policy would "choose" to do. Exploration is a non-issue for DP precisely because DP never relies on experienced samples in the first place — which is the same root cause (model-free vs model-based) behind nearly every DP/MC difference in Deep Dive A.

Research Enhanced

"ε-greedy isn't a hack bolted onto MC control — it's the mechanism that fulfills the same theoretical requirement (infinite visitation) that Exploring Starts satisfies in toy simulators. Because real environments can't be reset to arbitrary (s,a) pairs, ε-soft policies are the practical default, and GLIE schedules (εt→1/t→0) let the policy become truly greedy once exploration has done its job."

Deep Dive D

MC → TD: One More Step

Every MC method covered here — first-visit, every-visit, GPI with Q(s,a), ε-soft control — shares one trait: the update can only happen after the episode ends, because Gt isn't known until then. Temporal-Difference learning (Sessions 9-10) removes that wait by bootstrapping a guess of Gt from the very next step's reward and current value estimate — borrowing MC's "learn from sampled experience" and DP's "bootstrap off current estimates" at the same time.   • D. ρ replaces γ in off-policy MC — it is the off-policy discount factor.

Correct: A

On-policy MC (Sections 1-4's entire focus) learns about the same policy that is being used to generate experience. Off-policy MC separates the two: a behavior policy b generates the data, while a (possibly different) target policy π is the one being evaluated/improved. This only works under a coverage condition (π(a|s)>0 ⇒ b(a|s)>0 — anything π might do, b must have some chance of doing too). The importance sampling ratio ρt is a product of probability ratios π(a|s)/b(a|s) along the trajectory; multiplying an observed return by ρ reweights it to estimate what its "expected value under π" would have been, even though it was actually generated under b.

B is wrong — "off-policy" is about which policy generates the data vs which policy is being learned, not about online/offline connectivity to an environment. C is wrong — ρ is a continuous reweighting factor (it can take any positive value, e.g. 3.96 or 0.02 in the Section 5 worked example), not a binary keep/discard flag. D is wrong — ρ and γ serve completely different roles: γ discounts future rewards within a return; ρ reweights an entire trajectory's return to correct for sampling under the "wrong" policy. Both can appear in the same off-policy MC formula without one replacing the other.

See Section 5 (Appendix) — "The Gap: One Policy Generates Data, Another Wants to Learn," and the W→X→Y worked IS example.

Q. In a model-free MDP, explain why estimating only the state value vπ(s) is insufficient for selecting actions during control, even if vπ is estimated perfectly. (2–3 well-articulated statements; vague answers will be penalised.)

PE2 Easy 21-Dec-2025 · Q4(b) · 2 Marks

Q. In a deterministic policy that always selects the same action in a state, explain in 2–3 sentences why first-visit Monte Carlo estimation may fail to reach an optimal policy. Suggest one precise way to address this.

  1. A deterministic π(s) picks exactly one action per state, so every episode generated under π only ever visits the pairs (s, π(s)). For every other action a≠π(s), Q(s,a) never receives a return and stays frozen at its initial value.
  2. Policy improvement π′(s)=argmaxa Q(s,a) then compares one "real" (sampled) estimate against untouched initial guesses — if an untried action's initial Q happens to look highest (or the true-best action is simply never sampled), the policy can get permanently stuck on a suboptimal action.

Key: "maintaining exploration" (Section 4) is the single biggest conceptual hurdle in MC control — without it, first-visit MC's averages are computed over the wrong (s,a) pairs entirely.

Sample Q3 Medium exploring starts vs ε-soft 5 Marks

(a) [2] Why does pairing a fixed deterministic policy with first-visit MC not guarantee convergence to π*? (b) [1.5] Define "exploring starts" and give one reason it is often impractical outside a simulator. (c) [1.5] How does an ε-soft policy remove the need for exploring starts, and what is the GLIE trade-off involved?

(a) A deterministic π only ever generates trajectories through the (s,π(s)) pairs it already prefers. Any (s,a) with a≠π(s) is never sampled, so its Q(s,a) never updates — policy improvement (argmax over Q) can never discover that a different action would be better, and the policy can lock onto a local optimum permanently.

(b) Exploring starts: every episode begins at a (state, action) pair drawn so that every pair has non-zero probability of being the start, with the agent then following π for the rest of the episode. It's often impractical because in a real environment (not a simulator) you usually cannot reset the world to an arbitrary state on demand — e.g. you can't force a robot or a real user into an arbitrary mid-task configuration to "start" an episode there.

(c) An ε-soft policy keeps every action's probability ≥ ε/|A| > 0 in every state, so over many episodes every (s,a) pair keeps being sampled — the same coverage guarantee exploring starts gave, but achieved through the policy itself rather than through artificial episode starts. GLIE trade-off: to converge to the truly optimal (greedy) policy, ε must eventually → 0 ("greedy in the limit"), while still exploring infinitely often along the way — too-fast decay risks premature convergence to a suboptimal policy; too-slow decay wastes performance on permanent exploration.

Sample Q6 Easy concepts 5 Marks

Q. Short answers. (a) [1.5] Why does MC control estimate Q(s,a) rather than V(s), and how does this feed policy improvement? (b) [1.5] What does "maintaining exploration" mean for MC control, and name the two standard solutions. (c) [1] One-line difference between first-visit and every-visit MC. (d) [1] True/False with reason: "MC methods cannot update any value until the episode terminates."

(a) Acting from V(s) requires a model (one-step lookahead over p(s′,r|s,a)) to compare actions. Q(s,a) already conditions on the action, so policy improvement is simply π′(s)=argmaxa Q(s,a) — no model needed. This is why MC control (Sections 3–4) tracks Q, while MC prediction (Section 2) can stop at V.

(b) "Maintaining exploration" means ensuring every (s,a) pair keeps being visited so its Q estimate keeps improving (and isn't permanently frozen at an initial guess). Standard solutions: exploring starts and ε-soft (e.g. ε-greedy) policies.

(c) First-visit MC averages the return following only the first occurrence of (s,a) in each episode; every-visit MC averages the return following every occurrence.

(d) True. Gt = Rt+1 + γRt+2 + … depends on all future rewards, which are only fully known once the episode ends — so MC updates happen episode-by-episode, never mid-episode. (This is exactly why MC is restricted to episodic tasks, in contrast to TD methods which bootstrap and can update every step.)
