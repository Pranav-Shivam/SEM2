01

Knowledge-based agents

foundation

Every knowledge-based agent centres on one component: a KB. Not a database of English sentences — a set of sentences in a formal knowledge-representation language.

OperationMeaningDatabase analogue
TELLInform the KB about a percept it just receivedINSERT
ASKQuery the KB for what action to take, or whether a fact holdsSELECT
UPDATERevise the KB with something newly inferredUPDATE
A sentence is knowledge expressed in a knowledge-representation language. An axiom is a sentence taken as given, not derived from others. Every knowledge-based agent, at its core, does exactly three things: tell, ask, or update — the CRUD of reasoning.
Where this shows up in the lab Prolog — a logic-programming language built for exactly this — is covered in the coming webinar. You write facts and rules using AND/OR directly, then ask queries and get true/false back. Higher-level languages can do it, but it's clumsy; Prolog is built for it.
Research notes — beyond the slides The loop has a second TELL. AIMA's KB-agent cycle is TELL(percept) → ASK(action) → TELL(action) → execute — "the second TELL is necessary to let the knowledge base know that the hypothetical action has actually been executed." Without it the KB can't track its own location, arrow, or time step. Students routinely list only one TELL; the second one earns marks.

The knowledge level (Newell, 1982). A KB agent can be described purely by what it knows and wants, independent of implementation — "it doesn't matter whether its geographical knowledge is implemented as linked lists or pixel maps." That's the declarative approach; the procedural approach hard-codes behaviours directly. The 1970s–80s fought heated wars over which is right — AIMA's verdict: "a successful agent must combine both declarative and procedural elements," even compiling declarative knowledge into reflexes once inferences become routine.
02

The Wumpus World

the running example

A 4×4 grid. The agent starts at (1,1) facing right. Somewhere in the grid: pits, one wumpus, one gold. The goal isn't just grabbing the gold — it's discovering where the danger is before ever stepping near it.

FeatureRule
PitFalling in kills the agent. Every cell adjacent to a pit (up/down/left/right) feels breeze.
WumpusOne only, static, eats the agent on contact. Every adjacent cell feels stench.
GoldOne only. Glitter is perceived only in the same cell — it does not radiate outward.
PerceptsAlways a 5-tuple: [Stench?, Breeze?, Glitter?, Bump?, Scream?] — e.g. [Stench, Breeze, None, None, None]. Sensed locally, one cell at a time. Bump = walked into a wall; Scream = the wumpus died, heard from anywhere.
ActuatorsForward, TurnLeft / TurnRight by 90°, Grab (gold in this cell), Shoot (the single arrow flies straight until it hits a wall or kills the wumpus), Climb (exit the cave — only from (1,1)).
Performance measure+1000 climbing out with the gold · −1000 falling into a pit or being eaten · −1 per action · −10 for using the arrow.
Environment propertyAnswerWhy
Fully observable?NoOnly the current cell's percepts are known — never the full board
Deterministic?YesOutcomes are exact: step into a pit, you fall, every time
Episodic or sequential?SequentialEach action affects what's rational to do next
Static?YesPits, wumpus, and gold never move once placed
Discrete?YesA finite grid, finite actions per step
Single or multi-agent?SingleThe wumpus is treated as a hazard of nature, not a competing agent
The goal, precisely "Discover the location of wumpus, pits and gold so the agent can navigate to gold without falling prey to either." Grabbing gold is a search problem. Reasoning your way to that knowledge, one cell at a time, without needing to physically visit every square, is what this whole module is about.
Two classification traps (from the official AIMA answer key) It IS single-agent — the wumpus never decides anything; Russell's own slides call it "essentially a natural feature", terrain with teeth. It IS deterministic — every action's outcome is exactly specified. The world only feels random to the agent because of partial observability; "the environment is stochastic" is the classic wrong answer. Uncertainty about the world ≠ randomness in the world.
Research notes — where this game came from Gregory Yob wrote Hunt the Wumpus in BASIC in 1972–73 (published in Creative Computing, 1975) — and he built it specifically because he was bored of hide-and-seek games on square grids: his cave was a dodecahedron, 20 rooms with 3 tunnels each. AIMA's authors note the irony of putting it "back in the boring old grid." Michael Genesereth (Stanford) was the first to suggest it as an AI agent testbed. One more AIMA fact worth knowing: about 21% of randomly generated wumpus worlds are utterly unfair — the gold sits in a pit or is surrounded by pits — so even a perfect logical agent can't win them all.

04

Formalizing the rules — why AND, why OR

the subtle part

Two directions of the same relationship look almost identical on the page — and use opposite connectives. This is the single most common place to slip.

Pit(x,y) ⇒ Breeze(x+1,y) ∧ Breeze(x−1,y) ∧ Breeze(x,y+1) ∧ Breeze(x,y−1) forward direction — a pit guarantees breeze in ALL four neighbours, so it's AND
Breeze(x,y) ⇒ Pit(x+1,y) ∨ Pit(x−1,y) ∨ Pit(x,y+1) ∨ Pit(x,y−1) backward direction — a felt breeze only means SOME neighbour has a pit, so it's OR
Wumpus(x,y) ⇒ Stench(x+1,y) ∧ Stench(x−1,y) ∧ Stench(x,y+1) ∧ Stench(x,y−1)
Stench(x,y) ⇒ Wumpus(x+1,y) ∨ Wumpus(x−1,y) ∨ Wumpus(x,y+1) ∨ Wumpus(x,y−1)
Why the connective flips. If a pit exists, breeze in all four neighbours is guaranteed — certain, so AND. But standing in a breezy cell, the pit could be in any one of up to four places — you don't know which, so it's OR. Confusing these two directions is the single easiest way to get a Wumpus World question wrong.

What is logic?

definitions

Combine the current percept with the existing KB, reason according to the syntax and semantics of a formal language — that's logic, formally stated.

TermMeaning
SyntaxThe grammar of well-formed sentences — like x + 4 = 4 being valid arithmetic but xy 4 = 2 not being
SemanticsThe meaning — what makes a sentence true or false in a given world
ModelOne possible assignment of truth values to every variable — not necessarily reality, just one consistent possibility
Entailment (⊨)KB ⊨ α means α is true in every model where KB is true
The one rule underneath all of it: every sentence in propositional logic must be assessable as strictly true or false — never both, never neither. A false statement is still perfectly valid logic; an unanswerable one is not logic at all.
M(α) — the model-set notation from the slides With two sentences S1, S2 there are 2² = 4 models, m1…m4. M(S1) is the set of models that satisfy S1 — say M(S1) = {m1, m3}. Entailment restated in this notation: KB ⊨ α exactly when M(KB) ⊆ M(α) — every KB-satisfying model is also an α-satisfying model. The slides run a small version of this at (2,1) with breeze: three unknown neighbour cells → 2³ = 8 candidate models to reason over.
StatementA proposition?Why
1 + 1 = 2YesAssessable — and true
1 + 2 = 5YesAssessable — and false. Falsity doesn't disqualify it.
"B is a vowel"YesFalse, but still a clean true/false claim
"The sun rises in the east and sets in the west"YesTrue, and assessable
"Will you go to office today?"NoA question has no truth value
"You are beautiful!"NoExclamatory — not truth-evaluable
Research notes — grounding: why does syntax in the head work in the world? The KB is "just syntax inside the agent's head" — so what connects it to reality? AIMA's answer, in two parts: percept sentences are grounded by the sensors themselves (the smell sensor is what makes the smell sentence mean something), and general rules like "wumpuses cause stench nearby" come from learning — which is fallible. AIMA's joke: maybe wumpuses don't smell on February 29 in leap years, "which is when they take their baths." If the KB is true in the real world and the inference is sound, every derived sentence is also true in the real world — that's the whole contract.

Precedence, not ambiguity ¬A ∧ B and ¬(A ∧ B) are different statements — the first negates only A before ANDing with B, the second ANDs first and negates the whole result. A missing parenthesis is exactly what makes a line genuinely ambiguous.
Why P ⇒ Q is true when P is false — the election example "If I am elected, I will lower taxes." If the politician does not get elected, voters have no expectation left to violate — the premise never fired, so the statement can't be judged false. That's the entire content of row 3 and row 4 above: whenever the premise is false, the implication is vacuously true, regardless of the consequent. (Recommended background reading: Rosen, Discrete Mathematics and Its Applications, Ch. 1.1–1.4 — propositional logic and equivalences.)
English phraseTranslationWhy students get it wrong

Research notes — the two named fallacies of implication Given p ⇒ q: concluding p from q is affirming the consequent ("paintings are art; this is art; ∴ it's a painting" — sculptures exist). Concluding ¬q from ¬p is denying the antecedent ("humans have brains; she's a dog; ∴ no brain"). The only equivalent rewrite is the contrapositive ¬q ⇒ ¬p — the converse (q ⇒ p) and inverse (¬p ⇒ ¬q) are different statements. Valid moves: affirm the antecedent (Modus Ponens) or deny the consequent (Modus Tollens).
Research notes — the five connectives sit among sixteen A 4-row truth table can be filled 2⁴ = 16 ways, so there are exactly 16 binary boolean functions — the course uses 5 of them. Any of them is buildable from tiny sets: {¬,∧}, {¬,∨}, {¬,⇒} are each functionally complete, and NAND alone (the Sheffer stroke, 1913 — Peirce knew it in 1880 and never published) or NOR alone suffice — the reason real chips can be fabricated entirely from NAND gates. XOR can never be complete: it's a linear function, and composing linear functions only ever yields linear functions — AND is out of reach. One parsing convention worth knowing: ⇒ is right-associative, so a⇒b⇒c reads a⇒(b⇒c) — and the grouping genuinely matters, unlike ∧ or ∨.

Encoding the Wumpus World as a knowledge base

worked example

A literal like P(1,2) means "there is a pit in (1,2)". The classic five-rule KB below is small enough to hand-trace, and entails more than it looks like it should.

R1:  ¬P(1,1)                                    # given: agent's start cell is safe
R2:  B(1,1) ⇔ (P(1,2) ∨ P(2,1))                 # breeze in 1,1 iff a neighbour has a pit
R3:  B(2,1) ⇔ (P(1,1) ∨ P(2,2) ∨ P(3,1))        # same rule, centred on 2,1
R4:  ¬B(1,1)                                    # perceived: no breeze at 1,1
R5:  B(2,1)                                     # perceived: breeze at 2,1
Seven literals appear across R1–R5: P11, B11, P12, P21, B21, P22, P31. (Negation is an operator, not a new literal — don't double-count it.) Seven boolean variables means 2⁷ = 128 possible models to check by brute force.

The query on the table: does the KB entail ¬P(1,2) — is there provably no pit at (1,2)? Toggle the switches below to explore the space yourself, or jump straight to one of the three assignments that actually satisfy R1–R5.

¬P(1,2) and ¬P(2,1) entail — true in all 3 models. ¬P(2,2) does not — P22 is true in two of the three. Is this scalable? 128 rows is fine for a machine, brutal by hand, and this KB only had 7 variables. Formally: n symbols means O(2ⁿ) time, though only O(n) space if models are enumerated depth-first. That gap — correct but impractical — is exactly why the deck moves on to theorem proving.

Research notes — three symbols students merge into one α ⇒ β is a sentence in the language — just a connective, evaluated per row. KB ⊨ α is a statement about all models — semantic entailment. KB ⊢ α says a specific procedure derived α from KB — pure syntax, no models consulted. Soundness is "⊢ implies ⊨"; completeness is "⊨ implies ⊢". The deduction theorem is the bridge between the first two — and it "was known to the ancient Greeks."
Research notes — monotonicity, and a famous complexity fact Propositional logic is monotonic: if KB ⊨ α, then KB ∧ β ⊨ α for any β — new facts add conclusions, never retract them. Learning "there are exactly eight pits" can't un-prove ¬P(1,2). Human reasoning isn't like this ("birds fly" dies when the bird is a penguin) — that's what non-monotonic logics model. And the reason no method fixes the exponential wall: checking satisfiability (SAT) was the first problem ever proven NP-complete (Cook 1971, independently Levin — the Cook–Levin theorem); propositional entailment is co-NP-complete, and every known inference algorithm is worst-case exponential. The wall is fundamental; the tricks that follow just move it.

Theorem proving — equivalence laws & inference rules

the scalable alternative

The truth-table method is O(2ⁿ) — correct, but it doesn't scale. Theorem proving applies inference rules directly to the KB sentences, never consulting models at all. Two sentences are logically equivalent (α ≡ β) when they are true in exactly the same set of models — e.g. (P₁,₂ ∨ B₂,₁) ≡ (B₂,₁ ∨ P₁,₂).

#LawEquivalence
1–2Commutativity (of ∧, of ∨)α∧β ≡ β∧α  ·  α∨β ≡ β∨α
3–4Associativity (of ∧, of ∨)(α∧β)∧γ ≡ α∧(β∧γ)  ·  (α∨β)∨γ ≡ α∨(β∨γ)
5Double-negation elimination¬(¬α) ≡ α
6Contraposition(α ⇒ β) ≡ (¬β ⇒ ¬α)
7Implication elimination(α ⇒ β) ≡ (¬α ∨ β)
8Biconditional elimination(α ⇔ β) ≡ ((α ⇒ β) ∧ (β ⇒ α))
9–10De Morgan's laws¬(α∧β) ≡ (¬α∨¬β)  ·  ¬(α∨β) ≡ (¬α∧¬β)
11Distributivity of ∧ over ∨α∧(β∨γ) ≡ (α∧β)∨(α∧γ)
12Distributivity of ∨ over ∧α∨(β∧γ) ≡ (α∨β)∧(α∨γ)
Modus Ponens:  α ⇒ β,  α  ⊢  β "the mode that affirms" — "If I am tired, I need rest" + "I am tired" ∴ "I need rest"

But note the fragility: remove biconditional elimination from your rule set and this proof becomes unreachable — an ad-hoc collection of rules is only as complete as its weakest gap. That's AIMA's motivation for wanting one rule that is complete on its own: resolution, two sections down. (Recommended: Rosen, Discrete Mathematics and Its Applications, Ch. 1 through §1.4 — considered prerequisite knowledge for the program.)

CNF — the normal form resolution needs

memorize the 4 steps

Conjunctive Normal Form: a conjunction of one or more clauses, where each clause is a disjunction of literals — a "product of sums", e.g. (A ∨ B) ∧ (A ∨ B ∨ ¬C) ∧ ¬A. Resolution only operates on CNF, so every KB sentence gets converted first.

OrderEliminateStep nameR2 becomes…
1⇔Biconditional elimination(B11 ⇒ (P12∨P21)) ∧ ((P12∨P21) ⇒ B11)
2⇒Implication elimination(¬B11 ∨ P12 ∨ P21) ∧ (¬(P12∨P21) ∨ B11)
3¬De Morgan (push ¬ inward)(¬B11 ∨ P12 ∨ P21) ∧ ((¬P12 ∧ ¬P21) ∨ B11)
4∨/∧Distributivity of ∨ over ∧(¬B11 ∨ P12 ∨ P21) ∧ (¬P12 ∨ B11) ∧ (¬P21 ∨ B11)
The deck flags this order as a memorization target Biconditional elimination → Implication elimination → De Morgan → Distributivity. Always in that order — each step removes one kind of operator, and running them out of order re-introduces operators you already eliminated.

Running R2 and R3 through the four steps turns the five-rule KB into seven clauses. The slides restart the numbering at R6 here — these are CNF clauses, not the derived sentences of the proof above:

R1:   ¬P11                          # unchanged — already a clause
R6:   ¬B11 ∨ P12 ∨ P21              # from R2
R7:   ¬P12 ∨ B11                    # from R2
R8:   ¬P21 ∨ B11                    # from R2
R9:   ¬B21 ∨ P11 ∨ P22 ∨ P31        # from R3
R10:  ¬P11 ∨ B21                    # from R3
R11:  ¬P22 ∨ B21                    # from R3
R12:  ¬P31 ∨ B21                    # from R3
R4:   ¬B11                          # percept — already a clause
R5:   B21                           # percept — already a clause
Research notes — step 4 can explode, and how the real world dodges it The distributivity step is the dangerous one: a formula like (x₁∧y₁) ∨ (x₂∧y₂) ∨ … ∨ (xₙ∧yₙ) distributes into 2ⁿ clauses. Production SAT solvers never do this — they use the Tseitin transformation (1968): introduce one auxiliary variable per subformula, producing a CNF that is linear in size and equisatisfiable (satisfiable iff the original is) though not logically equivalent — exactly enough for refutation-style proving. For exam-sized formulas, distribute by hand; just know why nobody does at scale.

Resolution lab — derive the empty clause

interactive

Proof by contradiction: to prove ¬P(1,2), assume the opposite — add P12 to the clause pool — and resolve clauses until the empty clause {} appears. An empty clause is unsatisfiable, so the assumption was false, so the query is proven. Your turn: pick pairs of clauses that contain complementary literals (X in one, ¬X in the other) and resolve them.

Click any two clauses to resolve them 0 resolutions

—
The shortest path — two resolutions R4 (¬B11) + R7 (¬P12 ∨ B11) resolve on B11 → ¬P12. That resolvent + the assumption P12 resolve → {}. Contradiction reached, assumption destroyed, ¬P(1,2) entailed — the third and fastest of the three methods the deck applies to the exact same query (truth table, equivalence-law proof, resolution).
Watch for tautologies. Resolving R6 with R7 on P12 yields ¬B11 ∨ P21 ∨ B11 — always true, so it tells you nothing. A legal resolution isn't automatically a useful one; steering toward the empty clause is the skill.
Research notes — two rules beginners break Resolve on exactly ONE complementary pair. If two clauses share two pairs — (P ∨ Q) and (¬P ∨ ¬Q) — cancelling both at once gives {} and "proves" a satisfiable set contradictory: unsound. One pair at a time; the result there is a tautology, correctly useless. Factor the resolvent. Resolving (A ∨ B) with (A ∨ ¬B) gives A ∨ A → just A. Factoring isn't cosmetic — it's what keeps the set of derivable clauses finite, which is why PL-resolution always terminates (the ground resolution theorem: an unsatisfiable clause set always contains {} in its resolution closure).
Research notes — history and fine print The rule comes from J. Alan Robinson's 1965 paper "A Machine-Oriented Logic Based on the Resolution Principle" — one machine-friendly inference rule replacing the whole zoo of equivalence laws; it founded automated theorem proving and is Prolog's direct ancestor. Fine print #1: resolution is refutation-complete, not generatively complete — from A it will never volunteer A ∨ B, but asked "does A ∨ B hold?" it always answers. Fine print #2 (Haken, 1985): complete ≠ fast — pigeonhole formulas ("n+1 pigeons, n holes") force resolution proofs of size 2^Ω(n). A proof always exists; a short one is not guaranteed.
Research notes — Horn clauses, the bridge to the Prolog webinar A Horn clause has at most one positive literal — e.g. ¬P ∨ ¬Q ∨ R, which is exactly (P ∧ Q) ⇒ R. Exactly one positive = definite clause (head + body); none = goal clause. Why they matter: entailment on a Horn-clause KB is decidable in time linear in KB size via forward or backward chaining — no exponential wall at all. That restriction is precisely what Prolog implements (backward chaining over definite clauses), and it's why next session's forward/backward chaining exists as a topic.

DPLL — satisfiability with a brain

algorithm

Davis–Putnam–Logemann–Loveland: a complete, backtracking search over truth assignments that decides whether a CNF formula is satisfiable. Same worst case as the truth table — but three heuristics make it dramatically faster in practice.

HeuristicWhat it does
Early terminationA clause is true as soon as any one literal is true; the sentence is false as soon as any clause is false. Decide without completing the assignment.
Pure symbolA symbol appearing with the same sign in every clause can be assigned to make those clauses true immediately — no branching needed.
Unit clauseA clause with exactly one unassigned literal forces that literal's value. Each forced value can create new unit clauses — a cascade called unit propagation.
Unit propagation on the Wumpus clauses R4 says B11 = false. That instantly turns R7 (¬P12 ∨ B11) and R8 (¬P21 ∨ B11) into unit clauses, forcing P12 = false and P21 = false — the same ¬P(1,2) conclusion, with zero search. This is exactly the slide-55 example.
Research notes — why it's called DPLL and not DP Davis–Putnam (1960) eliminated variables by generating all resolvents — and blew up 1960s memory exponentially. Davis, Logemann and Loveland (1962) — the two programmers who hit that wall — replaced elimination with a splitting rule: pick a variable, try true, backtrack and try false. Worst-case memory dropped to linear, and the backtracking-search skeleton every modern solver still uses was born. AIMA lists five more scaling tricks beyond the three heuristics: component analysis, variable/value ordering, intelligent backtracking (backjumping + clause learning), random restarts, and clever indexing.

Research notes — the SAT revolution, in three facts 1. Modern descendants add conflict-driven clause learning (GRASP 1996 → Chaff 2001, which contributed the VSIDS heuristic and watched literals → MiniSat 2003), and routinely decide industrial formulas with millions of variables — NP-completeness notwithstanding; the annual SAT Competition has driven progress since 2002. 2. The incomplete alternative, WalkSAT (stochastic local search: pick an unsatisfied clause, flip a variable), often finds models faster — but can never prove UNSAT; silence tells you nothing. 3. Random 3-SAT has a phase transition at clause/variable ratio ≈ 4.27: below it almost everything is satisfiable (easy), above it almost nothing is (easy again), and hardness peaks exactly at the boundary — the famous easy-hard-easy pattern.

Sample exam problem — the online-shopping KB

exam pattern

Slide 56 gives this unsolved — it's the deck's template for how this module gets examined: natural language → propositional KB → CNF → DPLL → resolution proof.

The scenario, as given "If a customer frequently purchases electronics items, they are considered tech-savvy. Not all tech-savvy customers make expensive purchases, but all expensive purchases are made by tech-savvy customers. Tech-savvy customers always follow the latest trends. Customers who follow tech trends will receive recommendations for new electronic products."

(a) Represent the KB in propositional logic (no quantifiers).  (b) Convert to CNF and find one complete satisfying assignment (BSAT) via DPLL.  (c) Given ¬T with 100% certainty — will the customer receive recommendations? Prove by contradiction using resolution.
One valid solution path (a) Symbols: E = buys electronics frequently, T = tech-savvy, X = expensive purchase, F = follows trends, R = receives recommendations. KB: E ⇒ T, X ⇒ T, T ⇒ F, F ⇒ R. ("Not all tech-savvy make expensive purchases" blocks T ⇒ X — it adds no clause.)
(b) CNF via implication elimination: ¬E∨T · ¬X∨T · ¬T∨F · ¬F∨R. All-false — E=X=T=F=R=false — satisfies every clause (each has a true negative literal): one complete BSAT solution.
(c) Add ¬T, assume ¬R, try to refute: ¬R + ¬F∨R → ¬F; ¬F + ¬T∨F → ¬T — which merely agrees with what's given. No empty clause exists: assignment all-false satisfies KB ∧ ¬T ∧ ¬R. So R is not entailed — a non-tech-savvy customer is not guaranteed recommendations. Note the trap: ¬T doesn't prove ¬R either; R is simply undetermined, exactly like P(2,2) in the truth-table section.

Propositional logic — pros & cons

the verdict

Why the deck ends here — and why the agenda's last two items (predicate logic, forward/backward chaining) spill into the next session.

Pros ☺ Declarative — state what's true, not how to compute it. Handles partial, disjunctive, and negated information, which databases and plain data structures can't. Compositional — the meaning of B₁,₁ ∧ P₁,₂ derives from the meaning of its parts. Meaning is context-independent, unlike natural language.
Cons ☹ Very limited expressive power. There is no way to say "pits cause breezes in adjacent squares" once — it takes one sentence per square. "Some students are brilliant" can't be said at all. That's the gap predicate logic (with quantifiers) exists to close — previewed on the agenda, covered next session along with forward & backward chaining.
