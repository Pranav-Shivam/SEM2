This course is fundamentally about designing rational agents — programs that perceive an environment and act to achieve the best expected outcome. Session 1 sets up the vocabulary: what we even mean by "intelligence", the four classic ways to define AI, and the agent/PEAS framing used for the rest of the course.

What is intelligence?

There is no single accepted definition. Working description: the capacity for learning, reasoning, understanding, and the ability to perceive and act in the world. It shows up as reasoning (proving theorems, diagnosis), planning (taking decisions), learning & adaptation (recommending movies, learning traffic), and understanding (text, speech, scenes).

Intuition
Don't anchor on "human-like". Humans aren't always intelligent, and non-human behaviour can be intelligent. AI asks a sharper question: given what an agent perceives, did it do the right thing?

(1) Thinking humanly — cognitive modelling

Build systems whose internal reasoning matches how humans think. We probe human thought via introspection, psychological experiments, and brain imaging. Example: Newell & Simon's General Problem Solver (1961) — the first system to separate knowledge base from inference, and it was judged by comparing its reasoning trace to human subjects'.

Analogy from the slides
"Aeronautical engineering texts don't define their goal as making machines that fly so exactly like pigeons that they can fool other pigeons." Likewise, Deep Blue beats humans at chess without thinking like one.

(2) Acting humanly — the Turing Test

A machine is "intelligent" if an interrogator, exchanging text through a wall, cannot tell whether replies come from a human or a computer. To pass it needs: NLP, knowledge representation, automated reasoning, machine learning (+ computer vision & robotics for the Total Turing Test).

  • Loebner Prize — annual restricted-conversation contest.
  • Eugene Goostman — chatbot posing as a 13-year-old Ukrainian boy; first to "pass" (fooled 10 of 30 judges).
  • Problem: results are not reproducible, constructive, or mathematically analysable. And humans can be mistaken for machines (e.g. a savant like Shakuntala Devi).

(3) Thinking rationally — laws of thought

Aristotle's syllogisms codified correct argument structures (3rd century BC). "All boring classes → students sleep; this is a boring class; therefore students sleep here." Formal logic became the basis of rational thinking. Two problems: (a) not all intelligent behaviour is logical deliberation — reflexes aren't; (b) it's computationally explosive — even a few hundred facts don't scale.

(4) Acting rationally — the rational agent (our focus)

An agent perceives and acts. A rational agent acts to achieve the best outcome, or the best expected outcome under uncertainty. Rationality includes correct inference, but also acting without inference (a reflex — pulling back from a hot stove) and acting when no provably-correct inference exists.

Rationality, omniscience & bounded rationality

Ideal rational agent
"For each possible percept sequence, it does whatever action is expected to maximize its performance measure, given the evidence perceived so far and built-in knowledge."
  • Rationality ≠ omniscience. An omniscient agent knows the actual outcome; a rational one only maximises expected outcome from what it can perceive.
  • Information gathering / exploration is rational — acting to improve future percepts.
  • Autonomy: behaviour driven by the agent's own experience (it can learn & adapt), not only by the designer's built-in knowledge.
  • Bounded rationality: perfect rationality is unachievable with finite compute — so we design the best program for the given resources.

Foundations of AI

AI borrows from many disciplines (Textbook §1.2):

Philosophy — logic & reasoning, mind as a rule-governed physical state, foundations of learning & rationality.
Mathematics — formal logic, computation & algorithms, probability.
Economics — rational decisions, decision theory + probability, game theory, Markov decision processes.
Neuroscience — how brains function and compare to computers.
Psychology — cognitive psychology & cognitive science: the brain as an information processor.
Computer science / Control theory / Linguistics — building powerful machines; feedback & cybernetics; language & meaning.

Risks of AI

A favourite short-answer / "identify two risks" exam target.

Lethal autonomous weapons — locate, select and kill targets with no human in the loop; the danger is scalability.
Surveillance & persuasion — mass surveillance; tailoring information flows to modify political behaviour.
Biased decision-making — parole/loan models encoding race/gender bias; data reflects societal bias.
Impact on employment — shifts wealth from labour to capital; can worsen inequality even as total wealth rises.
Safety-critical use — driving, water systems: needs engineering/ethical standards on par with other safety fields.
Cybersecurity — defends against attacks but also powers automated phishing/blackmail; plus deepfakes, sustainability.

Agents & environments

An agent perceives its environment through sensors and acts through actuators.

Human agent
Sensors: eyes, ears. Actuators: hands, legs, mouth.
Robotic agent
Sensors: cameras, range finders. Actuators: motors.
Software agent
Senses data, reasons about goals, executes actions.
The agent function maps percept histories to actions: \( f : P^{*} \rightarrow A \). The agent program realises \(f\) on hardware: agent = architecture + program.

Vacuum-cleaner agent. Percepts = [location, contents] e.g. [A, Dirty]; Actions = Left, Right, Suck, NoOp.

AI agent vs Agentic AI
In this course an "agent" just means a rational agent (we don't care if it's a robot or an LLM). The difference between an AI agent and Agentic AI is the level of autonomy: a scripted FAQ bot is an AI agent; a network of bots that escalate, analyse sentiment, and orchestrate sub-tasks they learned to decompose is Agentic AI.

PEAS — specifying a task environment

Before designing an agent, specify its task environment with PEAS:

PerformanceObjective success criteriaSafe, fast, legal, comfortable trip; maximise profit

ActuatorsHow it actsSteering, accelerator, brake, signal, horn, display

Exam tip
PEAS appears every paper (Q1). Write a concrete performance measure (not "works well"), list realistic sensors/actuators, and remember: the Performance measure belongs to the environment/designer, not the agent.

Worked examples

  • Performance: healthy patient, minimise costs, minimise lawsuits.
  • Environment: patient, hospital, staff.
  • Actuators: screen display (questions, tests, diagnoses, treatments, referrals).
  • Sensors: keyboard entry of symptoms, findings, patient answers.
  • Performance: percentage of parts in correct bins.
  • Environment: conveyor belt with parts, bins.
  • Actuators: jointed arm and hand.
  • Sensors: camera, joint-angle sensors.
  • Performance: improvement in student's writing accuracy/score, errors corrected, learning rate, user satisfaction.
  • Environment: the student, their documents, the language/grammar rules, the editing app.
  • Actuators: on-screen suggestions, highlights/underlines, explanations, corrected text, progress reports.
  • Sensors: keyboard input (the text being typed), cursor/selection, click feedback on accept/reject.

A TABLE-DRIVEN-AGENT stores an action for every possible percept sequence. It fails because:

  • The table is astronomically huge (percept sequences grow exponentially).
  • It takes far too long to build.
  • It has no autonomy — it can't handle anything not pre-entered.
  • Even with learning, it would take forever to learn every entry.

This motivates compact agent programs (reflex, model-based, goal-based, utility-based, learning) instead of explicit tables.

Self-test

Q1. This course is primarily about which view of AI?
The whole course is framed around designing rational agents — agents that act to maximise expected performance.
Q2. "Rationality is the same as omniscience." This is…
Omniscience knows the actual outcome; rationality only maximises the expected outcome given evidence and built-in knowledge.
Q3. In PEAS, the performance measure for a vacuum agent could be amount of dirt cleaned. Which letter does it correspond to?
P = Performance measure. The dirt vacuumed, time taken, electricity used and noise are all candidate performance measures.
Q4. Which is not a stated risk of AI in CS1?
The slides note AI shifts wealth from labour to capital and can worsen inequality — but also makes workers more productive/employable. "Guaranteed total job loss" is not the claim.
