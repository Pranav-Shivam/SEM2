02

Sequence-to-Sequence Models

Transcript

Encoder + decoder, defined by analogy

Every sequence model — autoencoders, RNNs, LSTMs, and now transformers — has two components: an encoder and a decoder. Prof's analogy: the encoder is you learning the multiplication table of 2. The decoder is the exam — someone asks "what is 2 × 3?" and you produce "6." The encoder compresses input into a representation; the decoder uses that representation, plus a question, to produce output.

Concretely for a sentence "I am teaching CV": each word is an input to the encoder. The encoder transforms the sequence into bits and bytes, and in the process produces a single context vector — a compressed summary of the whole input (what action is happening, what language it's in, etc). That context is handed to the decoder, which combines it with a specific query (e.g. "what am I doing?") to produce an answer ("teaching").

Worked example — translation pipeline

Input: "The green witch arrived" (English). Standard steps, common to RNNs and LSTMs alike:

  1. Tokenization

    Break the sentence into individual words/sub-words; each token gets a token ID (can be represented in hex, binary, or any base — the number itself is arbitrary, just an index).

  2. Embedding

    Each token ID is mapped to a vector — dimension can be 32, 64, 512, 1024, anything. This is what "embedding" means: a numeric, vector-space representation of a token.

  3. Encoding

    Embeddings flow through the encoder's hidden layers, producing one context vector for the whole input.

  4. Decoding, step by step

    The same context vector is sent to the decoder at every time step (D1, D2, D3... — unrolled time steps of one recurrent decoder, not stacked layers). Each step predicts the probability of the next output token, given that shared context and the previous output — a softmax over the entire vocabulary, like an election across every word in the dictionary where softmax converts raw scores into vote shares summing to 100%. (Precision note: feeding the context at every step is the Cho et al. 2014 variant; in Sutskever et al. 2014 the context vector only initializes the decoder's hidden state.)

The bottleneck — identified live by the class

One context vector, shared by everyone, with equal weight

  • No matter how long the sequence, there is exactly one context vector, and it is broadcast identically to every decoder layer. A 500-character sentence still compresses to one vector. (Student answers accepted live: "length of the sequence" and "context is small.")
  • Equal weighting of every word — the pronoun "I" gets the same influence on that single context as the semantically important word "teaching." NLP preprocessing tricks like stop-word removal and stemming exist precisely to fight this, by removing words that shouldn't matter as much.
  • Forgetting under length — Prof's live example: "I am teaching CV. I want to grow as a professor and give the best quality education to my pupils." Ask what the second word was, and even the speaker can't recall it instantly. As context grows, a single vector loses the earliest information; LSTMs' gated memory mitigates this somewhat but don't remove the fundamental one-context-vector ceiling.
S S Rama Kishore Namala, nailing the diagnosis live

"I think context is being fed to every output instead of leaving the unimportant ones." Prof's response: "Exactly, so Rama nailed it" — this single sentence is the entire motivation for attention.

Research Enhanced — the papers behind this section

The seq2seq architecture described here has two origin papers the lecture didn't name: Sutskever, Vinyals & Le, "Sequence to Sequence Learning with Neural Networks," NeurIPS 2014 (LSTM encoder-decoder) and Cho et al., "Learning Phrase Representations using RNN Encoder-Decoder," EMNLP 2014 — the paper that coined the term context vector and introduced the GRU. The bottleneck is not just anecdote, it was measured: Cho et al., "On the Properties of Neural Machine Translation," SSST-8 2014 showed BLEU translation scores collapse sharply once sentences exceed ~20–30 words without attention, and Bahdanau's Figure 2 shows the attention model (RNNsearch) holding flat exactly where the plain encoder-decoder (RNNencdec) falls off a cliff. One training detail worth knowing for exams: decoders are trained with teacher forcing — during training, each step receives the ground-truth previous token rather than the model's own (possibly wrong) previous prediction, which stabilizes and speeds up learning.

Also verified: AlexNet's 2012 result was 15.3% top-5 error against the runner-up's 26.2% — a ~11-point single-year gap, the sharpest discontinuity in the benchmark's history and the concrete version of the "ceiling breaks" pattern Prof narrates.

In Plain Words — layman anchors for this section

Hidden state = the running mental note you keep while reading a sentence word-by-word — updated after each word, holding everything you've decided to remember so far. Context vector = summarizing an entire movie into one tweet, then asking someone to answer any question about the movie from the tweet alone — that's exactly what the decoder is being asked to do. Embedding = GPS coordinates for words: words with similar meanings get nearby coordinates, so "king" and "queen" live on the same street.

03

The Attention Mechanism

Transcript Interactive

Naming it precisely — the "representational bottleneck" Slides

The slide deck gives Section 02's problem an exact name: the representational bottleneck. Requiring the context c to be nothing more than the encoder's final hidden state forces the entire source sentence — however long — through one fixed-size vector. The deck's diagram draws this literally: encoder units chain e→e→e→e→e, and that last unit (circled, labeled "bottleneck") is the only thing the decoder chain ever sees; dashed arrows from that single bottleneck node fan out identically to every decoder unit.

The deck also gives the without/with comparison as one formal line each — worth memorizing verbatim for exams:

Without vs. with attention — the decoder's hidden-state update rule
Without attention: hdt = g(ŷt-1, hdt-1, c) — same static c at every step t With attention: hdi = g(ŷi-1, hdi-1, ci) — a fresh c_i, function of ALL encoder states, at every step i

The only symbol that changes between the two equations is the context term — c becomes ci — but that one subscript is the entire fix: the decoder's hidden-state recurrence g is unchanged, only what it's allowed to look at per step changes.

Worked numeric illustration — "bank" under a static vs. dynamic context Slides

The deck attaches literal numbers to the "bank" ambiguity so it's not just hand-waving. With one static context (no attention), the model's sense distribution for "bank" sits wherever training-data frequency puts it — skewed financial regardless of sentence:

Sense of "bank"P (static context, no attention)P (dynamic c₁, for "bank of river")
financial0.90.1

A single fixed context cannot move those numbers no matter what follows "bank" in the sentence. A dynamic ci, recomputed per decoder step from the encoder states actually near "river," swings the distribution almost entirely onto the correct sense — this is the numeric version of the "he went to the bank... river bank" example above.

The fix: a dynamic, per-word context

Rather than one static context vector broadcast to every decoder layer, attention computes a variable context ci for each decoder step — dynamically derived from the previous or next words, not fixed at encoding time. Prof's disambiguation example: "He went to the bank to find his bank account was empty, then he went to the river bank and cried." The two "bank"s are morphologically identical tokens but semantically opposite. A dynamic context lets each occurrence derive its meaning from its own neighbors — "bank account" pulls the first toward finance; "river bank" pulls the second toward geography. A single shared context vector cannot do this.

Live follow-up question: which model family looks at both the previous and next words when building this context? Answer, confirmed by Prof: bidirectional encoders (BERT). Precision note on the framing: unmasked self-attention is naturally bidirectional — encoder-only models (BERT) simply use it as-is, while decoder-only models (the GPT family, most modern LLMs) deliberately apply a causal mask that blocks attention to future tokens so the model can't cheat while generating left-to-right. Bidirectionality isn't an exotic variant; one-directional attention is the explicitly imposed restriction.

Calculating attention, step by step

  1. Score = similarity via dot product

    Take one decoder hidden state and take the dot product against every encoder hidden state. High dot product ⇒ high similarity between the decoder's current "question" and that encoder position.

  2. Problem — unnormalized scores

    Raw dot products aren't bounded: one score might come out as 20, another 0.1, another 200. A single large value can dominate everything else even when the smaller values are still relevant. ("Classical ML problem — certain values are way greater than other values, and as a result our result is skewed.")

  3. Normalize with softmax → αij

    Apply softmax across all the raw scores for a given decoder position, producing a weight vector (e.g. 0.1, 0.3, ..., 0.7) that sums to 1 — this is the attention weight αij, telling you how much each encoder position matters for this specific decoder step.

  4. Weighted sum → context vector ci

    Multiply each αij by its corresponding encoder hidden state and sum — this produces a context vector that is different for every decoder position, unlike the old single shared vector.

Plus, a more powerful scoring function Slides — the deck adds one refinement to Step 1: instead of a bare dot product, parameterize the score with its own trainable weight matrix Ws: score(hdi-1, hej) = hdi-1 · Ws · hej. Ws is trained end-to-end along with everything else, and it gives the network the freedom to learn which aspects of decoder/encoder similarity actually matter for the task at hand, rather than trusting a raw, unweighted dot product. This is the direct ancestor of Q/K/V's learned projections in Section 04 — same idea (make the comparison learnable), one matrix earlier.

Attention — the mathematical formulation Prof derived on the whiteboard
Raw score: score(h_d, h_e) = h_d · h_e — dot product of one decoder hidden state with every encoder hidden state Normalized weight: α_ij = softmax(score(h_di, h_ej)) — across all j for a fixed decoder position i Dynamic context: c_i = Σ_j α_ij · h_ej — a different context vector for every decoder position i

Key Insight — this is exactly what Q&A live asked about

Bhawani asked: "do we learn alpha over time?" Yes and no, precisely: αij itself is computed (by softmax) at every forward pass, never stored — what's learned are the parameters producing the hidden states (the RNN weights here; W_Q/W_K in transformers) that the scores come from, trained end-to-end via backpropagation. This is attention in encoder-decoder cross-attention form (decoder attends to encoder) — Vaswani's team adapts the same idea into self-attention in the next section.

Research Enhanced — additive vs. multiplicative, and the word "alignment"

One historical precision the lecture skipped: the original attention paper (Bahdanau et al., 2014/ICLR 2015) did not score with a dot product — it used additive attention, a tiny learned MLP: score = vaᵀ·tanh(Wa[hd; he]). The dot-product (multiplicative) scoring taught here was introduced a year later by Luong et al., EMNLP 2015 as a faster, simpler alternative — and it's Luong's version that transformers scaled up. Bahdanau's paper also calls the scoring function an alignment model, and the αij matrix an alignment — exam questions often use this word; plotted as a heatmap it shows which source word each output word "looked at" (the paper's famous Figure 3, with English-French word alignments lighting up along the diagonal). One more distinction relevant to a CV course: softmax gives soft attention — a little of everything, fully differentiable, trainable by ordinary backprop; hard attention (Xu et al., "Show, Attend and Tell," ICML 2015) picks exactly one location and requires reinforcement-learning tricks to train.

In Plain Words — layman anchors for this section

Softmax = turns any list of scores into percentages that sum to 100% — raw votes into vote shares. Dot product as similarity = two arrows pointing the same way overlap a lot → big value; perpendicular arrows → zero. Attention weights α = a highlighter budget: exactly 100% of the ink, split across the source words — important words get thick highlight, filler words get almost none.

04

Self-Attention & Query / Key / Value

Transcript Slides

Definitions, precisely — attention vs. self-attention Slides

The deck states both definitions as one-liners worth quoting exactly:

Definitions (Vaswani et al., 2017, NeurIPS — "Attention Is All You Need")
Attention = the ability to compare an item of interest to a collection of other items in a way that reveals their relevance in the current context. Self-attention = the comparisons are to other elements within the SAME sequence; those comparisons compute the output for the current input.

The deck also states the transformer's headline architectural claim in three short bullets that are easy to lose under all the Q/K/V mechanics: transformers map sequences of input vectors to sequences of output vectors of the same length — n words in, n contextualized vectors out, never a different count. They're built from stacked transformer blocks, whose key ingredient is the self-attention layer ("self-attention allows a network to directly extract and use information from arbitrarily large contexts"). And critically, transformers are not based on recurrent connections — no ht waiting on ht-1 — so every position's computation is independent of every other position's, which is exactly what makes parallel implementation (and therefore scaling to huge models) possible, unlike an LSTM that must process token 47 only after token 46 finishes.

The unparameterized precursor — self-attention before Q/K/V is introduced Slides
score(x_i, x_j) = x_i · x_j — raw dot product between two input embeddings, no learned projection yet α_ij = softmax(score(x_i, x_j)) ∀ j ≤ i — causal: position i only compares against itself and earlier positions y_i = Σ_(j≤i) α_ij · x_j — weighted sum of the SAME embeddings used for scoring (no separate value vector yet)

This is the deck's bare-bones self-attention layer — each output box "bi" attends to input box 1 through i only (a triangular, causal fan-in: b1 sees x1; b2 sees x1,x2; ...; b5 sees x1...x5), producing one yi per position, all computable in parallel since none of the bi depend on each other's outputs. The very next step — the one Vaswani's team actually shipped — replaces the reused xi in both the scoring role and the value role with three separate learned projections. That's Q, K, V, covered next.

From cross-attention to self-attention

In 2017, Vaswani and team ("Attention Is All You Need") adapted the attention mechanism above into self-attention: instead of comparing a decoder state against encoder states, compare tokens within the same sequence to each other. Prof's example, referenced live: "The animal didn't cross the street because it was too tired." (Attribution check: this sentence is not in the "Attention Is All You Need" paper itself — it comes from Google's August 2017 Transformer announcement blog post by Jakob Uszkoreit, later popularized by Jay Alammar's Illustrated Transformer, and is routinely mis-attributed to the paper.) The word "it" needs to resolve to "animal," not "street" or "tired." Self-attention computes, for every word, a similarity score against every other word in the sequence — "it" · "animal" comes out highest, so "it" derives most of its contextual meaning from "animal."

Crucially: self-attention needs no labeled data and no defined end-task at the point attention is computed. "I do not have any kind of output vector or anything planned in the encoder state... within the sequence, 'am' derives its attention from 'I,' 'going' derives its attention from 'am' and 'I.'" Prof called this "more of an unsupervised kind of learning mechanism" — the encoder doesn't need to know what downstream task (translation, classification, Q&A) it will ultimately serve. Precision note: self-attention is an architectural mechanism, not a learning paradigm — W_Q/W_K/W_V still get trained by whatever objective the network has (the original Transformer was fully supervised translation). What Prof gestures at is that attention needs no attention-level labels, and that this property later enabled self-supervised pretraining (BERT's masked words, GPT's next-token prediction) — "self-supervised" is the exact term, not "unsupervised."

Prof, clarifying attention vs. self-attention (asked twice, live)

"Self-attention is just an implementation of attention. Attention is a mathematical concept... Vaswani and his team took that same concept and said I can create variable context out of it... rather than [comparing] a decoder state to an encoder state, you look within the sequence itself."

Query, Key, Value — the YouTube search analogy

Vaswani's team operationalized self-attention through three learned vectors per token: Query (Q), Key (K), and Value (V). Prof's analogy: searching YouTube for "transformers deep learning."

VectorYouTube analogyTransformer role
QueryWhat you typed into the search barWhat the current token is "asking for," contextually

Prof's exam-answer framing: "First and foremost, we calculate the similarity between all the keys that are available and the query" — same dot-product-then-softmax recipe as plain attention, just renamed and formalized. Prof's information-retrieval framing (in response to a direct question): you don't scan a textbook slide-by-slide for "image classification" — you jump straight to the section header (the key) that you already know holds the answer (the value). That's what Q/K/V similarity is doing mathematically.

Scaled dot-product self-attention — Vaswani et al., 2017
Q = X·W_Q   K = X·W_K   V = X·W_V — learned weight matrices, multiplied with input embedding X Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax( Q·Kᵀ / √d_k ) · V √d_k scales down the raw dot product — "very similar to batch normalization... doing the same job" of stopping one large value from dominating.
Correction — what √d_k actually does (the paper's own words)

Prof's batch-norm comparison captures the spirit (tame large values) but not the mechanism: batch norm is a learned normalization layer using per-batch statistics, while √d_k is a fixed constant rescale. The paper's stated reason (§3.2.1): "for large values of d_k, the dot products grow large in magnitude, pushing the softmax function into regions where it has extremely small gradients" — a near-one-hot softmax whose gradients vanish. Dividing by √d_k keeps score variance ~1 regardless of dimension, keeping softmax in its trainable zone. Exam-safe answer: gradient health of softmax, not normalization statistics.

Live Q&A — Bhawani's question, and where students landed

"Isn't query the input itself, or is input different from query?" Prof: no — Q, K, and V are each produced by multiplying the input embedding with a separate learned weight matrix (W_Q, W_K, W_V). None of them are the raw input; all three (and the weight matrices that produce them) are trained jointly with the rest of the network. Sujoy Saha's later question — whether multi-headed attention shares one Q/K/V set or uses separate ones per head — Prof confirmed: every head gets its own separate Q, K, and V vectors, never shared.

In Plain Words — why three matrices, and what Q·Kᵀ really computes

Why not compare raw embeddings directly? Because one word needs to wear three different hats: how it asks ("what am I looking for?" — Q), how it advertises itself to others ("how should you find me?" — K), and what it actually contributes once matched ("here's my content" — V). The same person phrases a question differently than they'd answer one — three learned projections let one embedding play all three roles. Q·Kᵀ in one shot = every query dot-producted against every key simultaneously — a full n×n "compatibility scoreboard" of all word pairs. That n×n scoreboard is also the transformer's Achilles heel: comparing every token to every other token means cost grows quadratically with sequence length — O(n²) — covered under Limitations (Section 10). Defining self- vs cross-attention in one line: self-attention = Q, K, V all derived from the same sequence X; cross-attention = Q from the decoder, K and V from the encoder (Section 03's original formulation).

Fully worked example — computing y₃ for "I am studying" via causal self-attention Slides

The single most exam-relevant walkthrough in the deck: three tokens, x₁="I", x₂="am", x₃="studying". We compute the third output vector y₃ end to end. Because attention is causal here, y₃ is allowed to look at positions 1, 2, and 3 — not position 4 onward (there isn't one, but the rule is the same one used for longer sequences).

  1. Generate Q, K, V for every token. Each xi is multiplied by the same three learned matrices: qi=WQxi, ki=WKxi, vi=WVxi. This produces (q₁,k₁,v₁), (q₂,k₂,v₂), (q₃,k₃,v₃) — nine vectors total from three input embeddings.

  2. Compare q₃ against every key up to position 3. Since we're computing y₃, the query is q₃. Score it against k₁, k₂, k₃ (not k₄ or beyond — causal masking, Section 04's matrix view below): q₃·k₁/√d_k, q₃·k₂/√d_k, q₃·k₃/√d_k — three raw, scaled similarity scores.

  3. Softmax the three scores → α3,j. The deck's own worked numbers: softmax turns the three raw scores into α₃,₁=0.1 ("I"), α₃,₂=0.2 ("am"), α₃,₃=0.7 ("study") — summing to 1.0. Position 3 ("studying") ends up attending mostly to itself, with a smaller pull from "am" and the least from "I."

  4. Weight and sum the VALUE vectors (not the keys). y₃ = α₃,₁·v₁ + α₃,₂·v₂ + α₃,₃·v₃ = 0.1·v₁ + 0.2·v₂ + 0.7·v₃. Note precisely which vectors get summed here — the α's were computed by comparing queries to keys, but what actually gets blended into the output is the corresponding values. This Q-for-comparing/V-for-blending split is the entire reason Q/K/V are three separate matrices instead of one.

Result y₃ = 0.1·v₁ + 0.2·v₂ + 0.7·v₃ — a single output vector for position 3, computed independently of y₁ and y₂ (each of those ran its own qi-vs-keys-up-to-i comparison in parallel). Every yi in the sequence is produced this same way, simultaneously — this is exactly what "entire process can be parallelized, unlike RNNs which are sequential in nature" (the deck's own annotation) means in practice.
Common mistake to watch for

Summing the keys instead of the values in the last step, or forgetting the causal restriction and including k₄/v₄ (or beyond) when computing y₃ — causal self-attention at position i only ever sees j ≤ i, never future positions.

The matrix view of the same computation — QKᵀ with causal masking Slides

Doing the y₃ computation above for every position at once, across a slightly longer 5-token sequence, is exactly what SelfAttention(Q,K,V) = softmax(QKᵀ/√d_k)·V computes in one shot. Pack all N token embeddings into a matrix X ∈ ℝN×d (one row per token), then Q=XWQ, K=XWK, V=XWV. The product QKᵀ is an N×N matrix — row i, column j is exactly q_i·k_j. Causal masking sets every entry above the diagonal (future positions j > i) to −∞ before softmax, so softmax turns those into exactly 0:

The −∞ trick is what makes GPT-style decoder-only models causal: mask the upper triangle, and no amount of training can ever let position i peek at position j>i, because the gradient through those entries is always exactly zero.

Multi-Headed Attention

Transcript Interactive

The bottleneck of a single attention layer

One self-attention layer captures one "view" of the sentence. Prof's example — a sentence with three occurrences of the word "bank" (river bank, bank account, banking road) plus an implicit question "where is the user going?": a single attention layer can disambiguate the three banks semantically, or track where the user is going, but human comprehension does several of these analyses simultaneously ("three thought processes captured together"). One attention mechanism, run once, isn't enough to capture semantic disambiguation, grammatical correctness, and intent-tracking all at once.

The fix: rather than defining a separate encoder/decoder for each sub-task, run several self-attention layers in parallel — a multi-headed attention block. Each head is free to specialize on a different aspect: word-sense disambiguation, grammatical correctness, "where is the user going," tone, or anything else the data makes useful — Prof likened this to a "mixture of experts," and noted this specialization is learned, not hand-assigned during training.

Multi-head attention — the formal definition Slides
head_i = SelfAttention(Q_i, K_i, V_i) — one ordinary scaled dot-product attention per head i Q_i = X·W_i^Q   K_i = X·W_i^K   V_i = X·W_i^V — head i's OWN weight matrices, never shared with any other head MultiHeadAttention(X) = (head_1 ⊕ head_2 ⊕ ... ⊕ head_h) · W^O — ⊕ = concatenate along the feature dimension, then one shared output projection W^O
ComponentWhat it does

Every one of the h heads is provided its own independent set of Q/K/V weight matrices (Sujoy Saha's question, confirmed live) — this is what the formula's per-head subscript i on WiQ/WiK/WiV is doing. Vaswani et al.'s original configuration: d_model=512, h=8 heads, each head dimension d_k=d_v=512/8=64 — so concatenating 8 heads of 64 dims each reconstructs exactly 512 before WO is applied.

Live Q&A — combining heads: concatenate or add?

Bhawani asked whether aggregating across heads dilutes what each head individually learned. Prof's answer: the original Transformer paper concatenates head outputs (not averages/adds), which preserves each head's distinct signal rather than blending it away — the nanoGPT-style demo notebook shown live does exactly this. But he noted it's an architectural choice, not a law: some implementations use addition instead, purely as a compute/design trade-off, and it's the original-paper default (concatenation) that's taught as canonical.

Research Enhanced

The canonical multi-head formula is Vaswani et al., 2017: each head computes its own scaled dot-product attention over its own Q, K, V (each typically of reduced dimension d_model/h, e.g. 512/8=64), the h outputs are concatenated, then passed through one more learned linear projection W_O — concatenation-then-project, not simple averaging, is what lets the model recombine specialized per-head signals into one output vector without just diluting them. Interpretability work since then (e.g. Clark et al., "What Does BERT Look At?", ACL Workshop 2019) shows individual BERT heads do specialize in exactly the way Prof describes — some heads attend almost entirely to direct syntactic dependents, others to the previous token, others to what resembles coreference resolution (matching Prof's "it → animal" example) — providing empirical backing for the "mixture of experts" framing beyond analogy. Layman anchor for the concat + W_O step: eight specialists each write a short memo about the same sentence; W_O is the editor who merges eight memos into one coherent paragraph — nothing any specialist wrote gets averaged away before the editor sees it.

Positional Embeddings & the Transformer Block

Transcript

Why position must be injected explicitly

Self-attention has no built-in sense of order — unlike RNNs, which process tokens sequentially, a transformer looks at all tokens "at once." So the model needs to be told, separately, where each token sits in the sequence: a positional embedding. Prof: "if you say what is the meaning of the word at position 3, a transformer will be able to tell you" — but only because position was explicitly encoded and added to the token embedding, not because the architecture infers order on its own. Layman anchor: a book with its page numbers torn off and the pages shuffled — the transformer reads all pages simultaneously, so each page needs its number stamped back on. (Formal term: without positional information, self-attention is permutation-equivariant — shuffle the input tokens and the outputs shuffle identically, order invisible.)

Prof noted that major LLMs differ specifically in how they encode position — this is one of the real architectural differences between model families, not just branding. He referenced an LLM using rotary-style embeddings (the transcript's phonetic rendering doesn't match a real model name — almost certainly a mis-transcription of LLaMA, whose RoPE (Rotary Position Embedding) scheme, from Su et al., "RoFormer," 2021, is well documented) as differing from earlier GPT-style absolute positional embeddings, and noted Gemini and Claude/Anthropic use their own undisclosed schemes.

The lecture skipped the original scheme, which exams love: Vaswani et al. 2017 used sinusoidal positional encoding — fixed (not learned) sine/cosine waves of geometrically increasing wavelengths, PE(pos, 2i) = sin(pos/100002i/d), PE(pos, 2i+1) = cos(·). Each position gets a unique interference pattern of frequencies (like a clock with many hands moving at different speeds — the combination of hand angles uniquely identifies the time), and relative offsets become simple linear transforms, which is why it generalizes to sequence lengths never seen in training. The family tree: sinusoidal (fixed) → original Transformer; learned absolute → BERT, GPT-1/2/3, ViT; rotary (RoPE) → LLaMA, PaLM, most modern open LLMs.

Prof, on why embeddings themselves are a competitive moat

"None of these companies release their embeddings ever, because that is where the money lies." His "King" example: a rich embedding might encode gender, nationality, "has-a-queen," royalty — a poorly trained embedding of the same dimension can fail to capture those same latent attributes, even at identical vector size. Embedding quality, training data, and dimensionality all directly determine downstream model quality — two teams running "the same model" on differently-processed data will not get the same results, exactly as two students running the same ML algorithm on differently-preprocessed data get different scores.

Nuance on "never release embeddings"

True for closed frontier models (GPT-4/5-class, Gemini, Claude — internal weights including embeddings stay private), but open-weight models (LLaMA, Mistral, Gemma) ship their full embedding matrices publicly, and OpenAI/Google sell standalone embedding APIs (text-embedding-3, Gemini embeddings) as products. Prof's underlying point survives: embedding quality is a competitive asset — it's just monetized either as a moat (closed weights) or as a product (embedding APIs), not universally hidden.

Assembling the transformer block

  1. Input embedding + positional embedding

    Token embedding and positional embedding are combined by element-wise summation — the standard in the original Transformer, BERT, GPT, and ViT alike. (Concatenation is a rarely-used alternative; the transcript's "concatenated" phrasing is loose — summing is what every canonical architecture does.)

  2. Multi-headed self-attention

    As covered above — several parallel Q/K/V attention heads.

  3. Residual connection

    Add the block's input back to its output before normalizing. Sujoy Saha's answer, confirmed by Prof: this exists to address the vanishing-gradient problem and smooth the loss landscape — the same reason ResNet-101 uses skip connections. Layman anchor: a highway bypass — information and gradients can skip past each layer's "local roads," so the signal survives a 96-layer journey.

  4. Layer normalization

    Normalize activations at each layer — same motivation as batch norm, different mechanism: LayerNorm normalizes across the features of each token independently; BatchNorm normalizes across the batch. Transformers use LN because variable-length sequences and small/streaming batches make batch statistics unreliable.

  5. Feed-forward network (MLP)

    A position-wise fully-connected network applied after attention + norm, at every transformer block — typically expanding 4× (d_ff = 2048 vs d_model = 512 in the original paper): Linear → ReLU/GELU → Linear. Why it must exist: attention only mixes information between tokens (a weighted average — linear); the FFN is where each token gets its own nonlinear processing. Without it, stacked attention collapses toward one big linear operation. Layman anchor: attention is the meeting where tokens exchange notes; the FFN is each token going home to think it over alone.

  6. Stack N blocks, then softmax

    Multiple transformer blocks stack sequentially; the final layer produces a softmax distribution — over the vocabulary for language, or over classes for classification.

The transformer block, as two compact equations Slides
z = LayerNorm(x + SelfAttention(x)) — attention sub-layer + residual, then normalize y = LayerNorm(z + FFN(z)) — feed-forward sub-layer + residual, then normalize again
LayerNorm, expanded Slides
μ = (1/d_h) Σ x_i — mean, taken across the hidden dimension d_h of ONE token's vector (not across the batch) σ = √( (1/d_h) Σ (x_i − μ)² ) — standard deviation, same axis x̂ = (x − μ) / σ — normalize to zero mean, unit variance LayerNorm(x) = γ·x̂ + β — learned scale γ and shift β restore the model's freedom to un-normalize if that helps

The axis is the whole point: LayerNorm's μ and σ are computed per token, across that token's own d_h features — completely independent of every other token in the sequence and every other example in the batch. That's precisely what makes it robust to variable-length sequences and small/streaming batches, where BatchNorm's cross-batch statistics would be noisy or undefined.

Pre-LN vs Post-LN — a detail worth one line

The order described above (sub-layer → add residual → then normalize) is the original paper's Post-LN. GPT-2 and virtually every modern LLM moved the LayerNorm before each sub-layer (Pre-LN, formalized by Xiong et al., ICML 2020) because it keeps gradients stable in very deep stacks without the delicate learning-rate warm-up Post-LN requires.

Research Enhanced

Prof's aside — "we have actually run out of physical data in the world to train a transformer... right now we are training them using synthetic data, which will eventually corrupt it" — reflects a real, actively-debated concern. Villalobos et al., "Will We Run Out of Data?", arXiv 2022 (updated 2024; ICML 2024 position paper) estimated (80% confidence interval) the stock of public human-generated text could be fully utilized for training between 2026 and 2032 depending on compute-scaling assumptions. The corruption risk Prof flags has a formal name and mechanism: model collapse — Shumailov et al., "AI models collapse when trained on recursively generated data," Nature 631, 2024 (the earlier 2023 arXiv preprint carried the catchier title "The Curse of Recursion") shows training successive model generations increasingly on their own (or other models') synthetic output progressively narrows the tail of the data distribution, degrading diversity and factual accuracy over generations. Labs mitigate this today by mixing synthetic data with curated human data and strong filtering rather than avoiding synthetic data outright — but the underlying risk Prof names is a real, published finding, not speculation.

Transformers as language models Slides

The deck closes the pure-Transformer arc (before crossing into vision) with the architecture that everything above was building toward: an autoregressive language model. Input embeddings for a running sequence ("So", "long", "and", "thanks", "for", ...) each attend causally over every prior token (never future ones — the same −∞-masked triangle from Section 04), pass through a transformer block, then a final linear layer projects to vocabulary size, and softmax over the vocabulary turns that into a probability for every possible next word — "long," "and," "thanks," "for," "all," etc., each getting its own predicted probability at its position.

Language-modeling loss
L = (1/T) Σ_(t=1)^T L_CE — average cross-entropy loss over every position t in the sequence, exactly the same per-token softmax + −log(P(correct)) recipe as Section 02's seq2seq loss

This is precisely the recipe the nanoGPT-style live demo in Section 09 runs at toy scale: predict the next character, one causal position at a time, penalized by cross-entropy — and it is also, unmodified, the exact recipe ViT reuses in Section 07, just swapping "next word" for "class label" at the output head. Same block, same loss family, different head.

Vision Transformer (ViT)

Transcript Interactive

"CNNs are now Robin to Transformer's Batman"

The Vision Transformer paper (ICLR 2021) is, in Prof's words, the reason CNNs — while still important, especially as feature extractors — are no longer the default choice for state-of-the-art image classification. The core idea: treat an image exactly like a sentence. Break it into fixed-size patches, flatten each patch into a vector, add a positional embedding per patch, and feed the whole thing into a standard transformer encoder — the same architecture from Sections 04–06, unmodified. The paper's title is the layman explanation: "An Image Is Worth 16×16 Words" — cut the photo into jigsaw pieces and treat each piece as a word in a sentence.

From pixels to patch tokens — worked exactly as in lecture

  1. Split into 16×16 patches

    Whatever the image's overall dimensions, divide it into a grid of 16×16-pixel blocks — the patch size used throughout the original ViT paper (other variants use 14×14 or 32×32).

  2. Flatten each patch

    16 × 16 = 256 values per channel; for a 3-channel color image that's 256×3 = 768 values — flattened into one row vector, "awfully similar to your CSV kind of data."

  3. Add an absolute positional embedding per patch

    Patch 1, patch 2, patch 3... each gets a position index, just like word position in a sentence — otherwise the transformer has no way to know patch 1 is "the top-left of the image" versus anywhere else.

  4. Linear projection — required, not optional

    Correction to the transcript's framing: in the actual ViT paper (Eq. 1), the flattened patch is always multiplied by a trainable linear projection matrix E mapping it to the model dimension D — this is the patch embedding, the image analogue of a word-embedding lookup, never skipped. The 16×16×3 = 768 coincidence (which happens to equal ViT-Base's hidden dim) makes bare flattening look sufficient, but E is learned and required — and for ViT-Large/Huge, D ≠ 768 anyway. Layman anchor: not just unrolling the puzzle piece into a list of numbers, but passing it through a learned translator that rewrites raw pixels into the transformer's vocabulary. Prof's separate remark about using a CNN layer instead of a bare flatten is the paper's own hybrid ViT variant — ResNet feature maps as input tokens.

  5. Feed to a standard transformer encoder

    Once tokenized + positionally embedded, the transformer does not care whether the underlying modality was text, image, audio, or video — "any modality in this world can be passed through a transformer... and voila, I will give you a response."

The instructor's word-embedding analogy for patch projection Slides

The slide margin draws the patch-embedding step as a direct parallel to word embeddings in NLP, using the exact same running examples word2vec courses use — worth having ready because it's the single clearest mental model for "why does flattening a patch and multiplying by a learned matrix count as an embedding at all":

  • Vocabulary size ↔ patch count: a word-embedding table might index ~50,000 vocabulary words; a ViT's patch-embedding matrix plays the same indexing role for every possible flattened-patch input, except it's a continuous linear map (shared weights) rather than a discrete lookup table.
  • "King," "Queen" ↔ any patch: the classic word2vec example — nearby vectors for related concepts — is the same property a good patch embedding should have: two patches showing similar visual content (fur texture, sky, edge) should land near each other in the D-dimensional embedding space.
  • Named dimensions (Admin, Warrior, Gender, Royalty, Country, Death/Alive): the informal idea that individual embedding dimensions can (loosely) correspond to semantic attributes — the same intuition extends to patch embeddings, where individual learned dimensions may end up correlating with texture, edge orientation, or color statistics, even though nothing forces this interpretability.
  • "Bank = institution / road / river": the same polysemy example used throughout this guide, redeployed here to make the point that even patch tokens are context-dependent — a patch of grass could be "field," "golf course," or "park" depending on what the rest of the image (and self-attention) resolves it to.

Myth vs. reality Slides

"A computer vision model with no convolutions!" — not quite

The deck states ViT's headline marketing claim in green text, then immediately annotates its own claim in red ink with two corrections. Both are genuinely examinable, and both are a favorite "gotcha" question:

  • Patch embedding IS a convolution. With patch size p, splitting the image into non-overlapping p×p patches and linearly projecting each one to D dimensions is mathematically identical to a single Conv2D(kernel=p×p, in_channels=3, out_channels=D, stride=p) layer. Non-overlapping stride-p patches are exactly what a stride-p convolution produces — ViT's very first operation is a (single-layer) convolution, just not a deep stack of them.
  • The Transformer's MLPs are also convolutions. The position-wise feed-forward sublayer inside every transformer block applies the identical weights to every token/position independently — which is precisely what a 1×1 convolution does when applied across a spatial grid (or, here, a sequence). So the "no convolutions" claim survives only in a narrow sense: no multi-scale, overlapping, large-receptive-field convolutions of the CNN kind — but convolution-equivalent operations are present at both ends of the architecture.

Exam-safe framing: ViT removes the deep stack of convolutions that gives CNNs their inductive bias (see the inductive-bias discussion below) — it does not remove convolution-equivalent math entirely. "No convolutions" is a slogan, not a literal architectural fact.

ViT vs. ResNet — the actual numbers from lecture

Research Enhanced — citation and numbers checked

Dosovitskiy et al., "An Image Is Worth 16x16 Words: Transformers for Image Recognition at Scale," ICLR 2021 — the correct citation for the paper Prof describes. Its actual headline result: ViT-Huge/14 pretrained on the (then-proprietary) JFT-300M dataset reached 88.55% top-1 on ImageNet, matching or beating contemporary CNNs (BiT-L/Noisy Student ResNets, ~87–88%) — but critically, ViT underperformed ResNets of comparable size when pretrained only on ImageNet-1k itself; the data-hungry caveat Prof stresses is the paper's own headline finding, not an exaggeration. The gap closes with scale: Zhai et al., "Scaling Vision Transformers," CVPR 2022 pushed ViT to ~90.45% top-1 with a 2-billion-parameter model. Prof's ResNet ceiling (~85%) matches published ResNet-152/ResNeXt numbers with modern training recipes; his ViT ceiling (~90%) is consistent with scaled ViT variants, not the original base configuration alone. One scale correction: "1 trillion images" is hyperbole — JFT-300M is 300 million images, its successor JFT-3B is 3 billion; no vision dataset approaches a trillion. And the data-hunger story has a famous counterpunch worth knowing: Touvron et al., "DeiT," ICML 2021 trained ViT on ImageNet-1k alone — no JFT — to 83.1% top-1 (85.2% distilled) using a distillation token plus aggressive augmentation, proving the hunger is a training-recipe problem as much as a raw-data one.

Research Enhanced — the missing word: inductive bias

The ViT paper's own explanation for the data hunger — a term this lecture never used but exams almost certainly will: inductive bias. CNNs bake two assumptions directly into the architecture: locality (nearby pixels relate — enforced by small kernels) and translation equivariance (a cat detector works anywhere in the frame — enforced by weight sharing). ViT has neither built in; self-attention is global and position-agnostic from the start, so everything CNNs get for free must be learned from data. That is precisely why ViT loses to ResNets when pretrained only on ImageNet-1k and wins once pretrained on 300M+ images — the paper's central finding, restated: large-scale data beats hand-built inductive bias, but only once you actually have the data. Layman anchor: a CNN is born wearing glasses that only see small neighborhoods — a helpful prejudice; ViT is born with no assumptions at all and must be shown millions of images to learn what the CNN knew from birth.

Why This Matters — one architecture, every modality

"Any modality in this world can be passed by transformer and this is where transformers become what they are." Once tokenized + positionally embedded, a transformer is modality-agnostic — this is the architectural reason today's multimodal models (image-captioning, visual Q&A, text-to-image) exist at all; they're not stitched-together pipelines but one shared attention mechanism operating over mixed token streams.

Toy character-level transformer (nanoGPT-style)

Prof's first demo used a tiny character-level sequence ("cbab", "bc" etc.) to make every step of the pipeline visible at toy scale: each character → token ID (a=0, b=1, c=2...) → token embedding, concatenated with a positional embedding → 3 parallel multi-headed attention heads, each with its own Q/K/V → attention output → residual connection → feed-forward (MLP) → repeated across transformer blocks → final softmax over the character vocabulary, trained to predict the next character.

# Toy character-level transformer, as walked through live
tokens = {'a':0,'b':1,'c':2,'d':3, ...}          # simplistic char-level tokenization
tok_emb = embedding_table[tokens]                # learned token embedding
pos_emb = positional_table[range(seq_len)]       # learned/fixed positional embedding
x = tok_emb + pos_emb                            # combined input

for block in transformer_blocks:                 # e.g. 2 blocks in the toy demo
    attn_out = multi_head_attention(x, n_heads=3) # separate Q,K,V per head
    x = layer_norm(x + attn_out)                  # residual + norm
    ff_out = mlp(x)
    x = layer_norm(x + ff_out)                    # residual + norm

logits = final_linear(x)
probs = softmax(logits)                          # P(next char | context)
# Output on an untuned toy run: input "cbab" -> output drifts to "ccmbainai"
# first characters correct, later ones wander -- expected at this scale/training

ViT-from-scratch notebook (PyTorch), CIFAR-10-trained

The main event: a from-scratch PyTorch implementation of the original ViT paper (not calling a pre-built library transformer), so every tensor shape is visible and testable. Steps Prof walked through live:

  1. Patchify + flatten

    128×128 input image, 16×16 patches → 64 patches, each flattened to a 768-dim vector (256 pixels × 3 channels); Prof visualized 10 example flattened patches directly.

  2. Patch + positional embedding, concatenated

    Unit-tested live: "65 patches, 768 dimension, one" — confirming shapes before proceeding (64 patches + 1 class token).

  3. Custom self-attention class

    Written from scratch rather than using PyTorch's built-in attention — "a good exercise... it will resolve a lot of your issues." Defines Q, K, V as learned NumPy/Torch linear layers over the 768-dim embedding, computes Kᵀ·Q divided by √d_k, then softmax.

  4. Custom multi-headed attention class

    Splits embedding dim by number of heads, runs the self-attention class per head in parallel, concatenates, projects, and returns.

  5. Residual + MLP (feed-forward) block

    Attention output + residual → MLP → residual again, matching Section 06's transformer block recipe exactly.

  6. MLP classification head

    A dedicated output vector (the class token) is passed through a small MLP to predict the final class label — this is what makes ViT directly usable for classification out-of-the-box.

Key Insight — you don't retrain the encoder per task

Prof's closing point on the demo: once the ViT encoder has learned a good data representation, you can reuse it for other queries entirely — "fetch me all airplane images that you have" — without retraining the encoder. Only the small decoder/head needs to change per task. The standard vocabulary for this, worth having ready in an exam: transfer learning (reuse a pretrained model on a new task), a frozen backbone (encoder weights locked, untouched), a linear probe (train only a single linear layer on top of frozen features — the cheapest test of representation quality), and fine-tuning (unfreeze and continue training some or all encoder weights on the new task, at a low learning rate). Prof's "fetch me all airplane images without retraining" is a frozen-backbone retrieval scenario. One connection back to the demo: a from-scratch ViT on CIFAR-10 needs heavy augmentation precisely because, with no CNN inductive biases and only 50k images, it otherwise overfits — Section 07's data-hunger point playing out live at toy scale.

Limitations & Live Q&A

Transcript
Two limitations Prof named explicitly, twice

Data hunger and compute cost

  • Data hunger: transformers need orders of magnitude more training examples than CNNs to reach comparable or better accuracy — "you train it on 1 billion images, in fact 1 trillion images. That is the scale at which transformers learn."
  • Compute cost: "you are literally melting your GPUs just to run a normal transformer model" — and transformers are only economical at scale/high query volume, which is why GPU access has become a competitive bottleneck across the industry.

The mechanism behind "melting GPUs" — quadratic attention cost

The textbook limitation the lecture's compute point rests on, never named live: self-attention compares every token with every other token, so the attention score matrix is n×n — time and memory grow quadratically, O(n²), with sequence length. Double the sequence (or image resolution, since patches = tokens), quadruple the cost. Layman anchor: a room where everyone must shake hands with everyone — 10 people is 45 handshakes, 100 people is 4,950; double the room, quadruple the handshakes. This is the reason long documents and high-resolution images are hard for vanilla transformers, why LLM context windows are a headline spec, and what an entire efficiency literature attacks: Swin Transformer restricts attention to shifted local windows (cost linear in image size — only shake hands at your own table), and FlashAttention reorders the exact same math to be GPU-memory-friendly without approximating anything. Near-certain exam question: "state the computational complexity of self-attention with respect to sequence length, and why" — answer: O(n²), because the n×n score matrix requires every pairwise dot product.

Invariances — handled by data, not architecture

Raghunandan asked directly about translation/rotation invariance. Prof's answer: every neural network handles some baseline translational/rotational invariance, but for transformers specifically, robustness to viewpoint/orientation changes comes largely from data augmentation and volume — feeding millions of examples across different cat orientations, lighting, and poses — rather than being architecturally guaranteed the way, say, convolution's weight-sharing gives CNNs built-in translation equivariance.

Hallucination, defined the way Prof frames it

Bhawani's example, borrowed from Prof: ask an image model to "make me as tall as my wife" and you get a bizarre result — because the model has no real context for who "your wife" is; rather than refusing, it invents a plausible-looking substitute. Prof's definition: "this model is probabilistic in nature, and what you call as hallucination... there is a chance that if a model does not have context about a particular item, it may invent that context." Layman anchor: a student bluffing an exam answer — never writes "I don't know," always produces something fluent and plausible-looking, because it was only ever graded on producing likely-sounding continuations, never on admitting ignorance. His broader claim, offered as personal opinion rather than settled fact: transformers alone won't reach AGI, and the field may eventually move toward world models — but transformers remain the first architecture to genuinely unify multiple data modalities under one mechanism, "and it is good to understand it."

Research Enhanced

Prof's "world models" aside tracks a real, active research direction rather than idle speculation: Ha & Schmidhuber, "World Models," 2018 introduced the term for architectures that learn a compressed simulation of environment dynamics rather than only next-token statistics; more recently LeCun, "A Path Towards Autonomous Machine Intelligence," 2022 makes an explicit public argument that autoregressive LLMs/transformers are structurally unsuited to genuine world understanding and proposes JEPA-style predictive world models as an alternative research direction — this is a live, contested debate among senior researchers, not a settled consensus, and Prof's framing sits squarely inside it.

On hallucination specifically: the mechanism Prof describes — invented plausible content filling a context gap — matches the standard technical definition in the literature, e.g. Ji et al., "Survey of Hallucination in Natural Language Generation," ACM Computing Surveys, 2023, which frames hallucination as generated content that is fluent and plausible but unfaithful to the source/context, precisely because the model optimizes for likely-next-token continuation rather than verified factual grounding. The survey's useful split: intrinsic hallucination contradicts the given source; extrinsic hallucination adds content the source can't verify either way — Prof's "invented wife" example is extrinsic.
