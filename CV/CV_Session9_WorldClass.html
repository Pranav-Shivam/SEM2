01

The Classification Problem

Transcript

Why this session is a pivot

Everything up to Session 8 built features — edges, corners, SIFT keypoints, HoG descriptors, RANSAC-fitted models. Session 9 asks: now that you have features, how do you actually decide what the object is? This is where classical computer vision (90s–2000s) hands off to the deep learning arc. Prof was explicit: today repeats the pipeline you already know from ML — collect data, train, predict — because that repetition is what sets the context for how the field evolved into CNNs and eventually Vision Transformers.

Computer vision scientists build every algorithm in three stages, in this order: what (classification — is a dog present), where (detection/segmentation — where is the dog in the image), track (temporal — where is the dog going). Session 9 is entirely about the first stage.

Prof. Dhruba, on why computers struggle where humans don't

"For us, it is a lot more easier. For computers, it is particularly a tough problem." — on the shape/color distinctiveness challenge (a green tank against green background vs. a grasshopper on grass).

The recognition challenges (why this is hard)

These are the same "nuisance" factors introduced pre-mid-sem with SIFT, now re-applied to whole-image classification:

ChallengeProf's example

IlluminationSame object, different lighting → very different pixel intensities
DeformationCat sleeping on its back — hard to distinguish from a leopard's pose
OcclusionPredators are "very good in hiding" — subject partly hidden behind clutter
Intra-class variation"Not every dog is the same" — many breeds, one label

Why This Matters — this is the managed-service you already use

Prof cited Google Cloud Vision as a real product built on exactly this pipeline — upload an image, get back a label with a confidence score (e.g. "woodwind instrument," "flute"). Every cloud "auto-tag this photo" feature is image classification wearing a UI.

Research Enhanced

This exact challenge taxonomy — viewpoint, illumination, deformation, occlusion, intra-class variation, background clutter — traces to Stanford's CS231n image classification notes (Karpathy, Fei-Fei Li et al., cs231n.github.io/classification), not folklore; it's been copied into a decade of CV syllabi since. What's missing from the classic framing is the implied arc that better architectures would retire it. They haven't.

Foundation models still measurably degrade on these exact axes. A 2023 study of CLIP on real-world assistive-tech benchmarks (ORBIT, VizWiz — low-vision users' photos) found occlusion cut accuracy by 9–14 points, viewpoint by 8–9, and lighting by up to 23 points relative to clean images. SAM2 (Ravi et al., Meta, 2024) had to add a dedicated occlusion-prediction token/head to its architecture specifically because the original SAM couldn't reliably say an object was currently hidden — and even with the fix, SAM2 still documents failures on occlusion from clutter like branches and leaves. DINOv2 fares better on viewpoint/illumination invariance, but only because heavy multi-crop augmentation was deliberately baked into its training — a targeted fix, not evidence the problem dissolved on its own.

The dataset built to make this unambiguous is ObjectNet (Barbu et al., NeurIPS 2019): 50,000 images with viewpoint, rotation, and background deliberately randomized as experimental controls. Detectors that looked state-of-the-art on ImageNet/COCO dropped roughly 40–45% in accuracy the moment those three CS231n-named variables were decoupled from the correlations models had learned to exploit. The classic list wasn't a beginner's problem statement — it remains the benchmark.

Live Q&A — Deshmukh's question on multi-label ordering

Deshmukh asked whether multi-label classification must always run binary-then-multi-class, in that fixed order. Prof's answer: no — stage one can itself be either binary or multi-class, depending on the problem. Example given: a visual quality-inspection system first asks a binary question (defect / no defect), and only if "defect" does it run a second-stage multi-class model (scratch vs. dent vs. crack). "Depending on your problem statement, your multi-label will change." The two stages are not always binary→multiclass; sometimes it's multiclass→multiclass, chained however many times the problem needs.

Research Enhanced

Exact numbers behind the names: MNIST — 70,000 28×28 grayscale digit images, 60k train/10k test, remixed from NIST Special Databases 1 & 3 (LeCun, Bottou, Bengio & Haffner, Proc. IEEE, 1998). CIFAR-10 — 60,000 32×32 colour images, 10 classes, 6,000 each, drawn from the 80-Million Tiny Images corpus (Krizhevsky, 2009, supervised by Hinton). PASCAL VOC — ran as an annual challenge 2005–2012; VOC2012 has ~11,530 images, 27,450 annotated objects, 20 classes (Everingham et al., IJCV, 2010). ImageNet — 14M+ hand-labeled images across ~21,800 WordNet synsets (Deng, Dong, Socher, Li, Li & Fei-Fei Li, CVPR 2009); its ILSVRC subset (2010–2017) used 1,000 classes and ~1.28M training images. COCO — ~328,000 images, 2.5M labeled instances, 91 categories, emphasizing scene context over isolated objects (Lin et al., Microsoft, ECCV 2014).

The 2012 inflection point, precisely: AlexNet (Krizhevsky, Sutskever & Hinton, NeurIPS 2012) cut ILSVRC-2012 top-5 error to 15.3%, against 26.2% for the next-best entry — a hand-engineered SIFT+Fisher-vector pipeline. That ~11-point absolute drop is still the largest single-year jump in the challenge's history: an 8-layer CNN trained on two GTX 580 GPUs with ReLU and dropout, shrinking training time from weeks to about a week.

Where these stand in 2026: none are deprecated, but most are saturated relative to their original purpose. MNIST/CIFAR-10 now mainly serve as teaching/unit-test sets (models exceed ~99.7%/~99.5% accuracy). ImageNet-1k top-1 accuracy plateaued near 90–91% (e.g. CoCa, Google, 2022) — near estimated human performance — which is why ILSVRC itself ended in 2017; papers now report robustness variants (ImageNet-A/R/Sketch, ObjectNet) or foundation-model suites (VTAB, MMMU) instead of a bare top-1 number. COCO and PASCAL VOC remain standard for detection/segmentation, but COCO has effectively superseded VOC, and the harder long-tail LVIS dataset is increasingly cited alongside it for current SOTA detection claims.

03

Bag of Words & the Data-Driven Approach

Transcript

The data-driven approach, in two functions

Since no rule can be hand-coded for "catness," the field pivoted to supervised, data-driven learning: collect N images per label (different orientations, occlusions, illuminations — "the more the merrier"), then reduce the whole problem to two functions:

  1. train(images, labels) → classifier

    Extract features from every training image (edges, corners, HoG, SIFT — whatever you already know how to compute), feed them with their labels to a classifier algorithm, and store the learned parameters.

  2. predict(image) → label

    Extract the same features from a new image, run them through the learned classifier, and output a class score / probability.

Bag of Words — the first classification algorithm

Prof traced today's pipeline back to Bag of Words (Bo\W), first developed for computer vision before it became an NLP staple. The recipe:

  1. Detect key patches

    Blobs from SIFT — regions of interest rather than the whole image.

  2. Extract features per patch

    x, y, σ (scale), and principal orientation — i.e. the SIFT + HoG feature set you already built.

  3. Compute a histogram

    Flatten every patch's features into one row; stack rows across patches into a table (rows = keypoints, columns = feature dims) — "awfully similar to your CSV kind of data."

  4. Classify the histogram

    Feed the histogram table to any classifier — SVM, random forest, or (today's focus) nearest neighbor.

Attribution, as given in lecture (auto-transcript, unverified)

Prof credited this Bag-of-Words methodology to a paper he referred to (heard in the recording as "Sarska") published via Springer around 2006–2007, and said it originated in computer vision before NLP adopted the name. The reference book he named for full depth is Milan Sonka & Zelensky's textbook.

Research Enhanced — citation corrected

The "Sarska, Springer, 2006–2007" attribution doesn't correspond to any real paper in this literature. The correct originating work is Csurka, Dance, Fan, Willamowski & Bray, "Visual Categorization with Bags of Keypoints," ECCV Workshop on Statistical Learning in Computer Vision, Prague, 2004 — the paper that coined "bag of keypoints" and first paired SIFT-like local descriptors, k-means vector quantization into a visual vocabulary, and an SVM classifier over the resulting histogram. It builds directly on Sivic & Zisserman, "Video Google," ICCV 2003, which introduced the visual-word vocabulary and inverted-index retrieval a year earlier, for video retrieval rather than categorization. "Sarska" is almost certainly a mis-transcription of "Csurka."

The claimed direction is also reversed: bag-of-words originated in text/information retrieval, not computer vision. The term traces to distributional linguistics (Zellig Harris, 1954) and the vector-space model for document retrieval (Salton and colleagues, 1960s–70s), well before vision researchers adopted it — Sivic & Zisserman explicitly borrowed the text-retrieval analogy (word counts, inverted files, tf-idf-style weighting) to build "Video Google." CV imported the framework from NLP, not the other way around.

What replaced it: better feature-aggregation schemes — Fisher vectors (Perronnin & Dance, CVPR 2007) and VLAD (Jégou, Douze, Schmid & Pérez, CVPR 2010) — and then, from roughly 2012–2014 onward, end-to-end learned CNN embeddings (AlexNet and successors), which made hand-crafted SIFT-plus-quantization pipelines largely obsolete for classification by the mid-2010s.

Why This Matters — you already did this in Assignment 1

"You have taken a set of SIFT features or edges, corners or whatever be it. You have computed a histogram and then you have applied say SVM or say a random forest... and you have classified urban versus rural image." Session 9's theory is a formal name for the pipeline your own assignment already implemented.

04

Nearest Neighbor & K-Nearest Neighbors

Transcript CIFAR-10 demo

The core idea — distance as similarity

"Nearest neighbor algorithms — these are distance-based algorithms." Prof's framing example: given a human face, another human face, and a cat, the distance between the two human faces will always be smaller than the distance between either face and the cat. Classification becomes: find the closest match in a labeled dictionary and copy its label.

Training a K-NN model is literally just storing a dictionary — {label: [feature vectors of every training image with that label]}. There is no fitting, no optimization at train time.

K-NN — distance metrics
L1 / Manhattan (Hamming, in Prof's phrasing): d₁(I₁,I₂) = Σ |I₁ᵖ − I₂ᵖ| — pixelwise absolute difference, summed L2 / Euclidean: d₂(I₁,I₂) = √( Σ (I₁ᵖ − I₂ᵖ)² ) — square root of summed squared difference Decision rule: label(x) = label( argmin_i d(x, xᵢ) ) for K=1; for K>1, majority vote among the K closest

Worked walk-through, as Prof described it

Take a training set with images already labeled boat / dog / frog / cat / plane. An incoming test image is compared against every stored image, producing a distance score for each (e.g. 0.1, 0.01, 0.3, 0.99, ...). The closest score wins — "this image and this image, they are similar" — and the test image inherits that neighbor's label. Prof was explicit that the match need not be exact: a same-species image at a different scale, or a same-family aquatic animal in a different color, can still register as the closest match by a comfortable margin (e.g. 0.8 vs. the next-best 0.7).

Two core problems with K-NN

Both are exam-testable in one sentence each

  • Inverted time complexity: training is O(1) — you just record images, features, and labels. But inference is O(N) — one test image is compared against every stored training image (60,000 comparisons for CIFAR-10), then argmax over all distances. "Normally it needs to be opposite — your training time can be lengthy, but your inference time should be as fast and real-time as possible."
  • Poor generalization — pure intensity-to-intensity matching: shift an image by a few pixels, or occlude/paint part of a face, and raw K-NN (without SIFT/HoG on top) sees a completely different intensity pattern and fails, even though a human sees the same object instantly. "It is just computing matching distance to distance."
Prof. Dhruba, on the flipped-face example

"If I take my image right now, and I just flip the image... the images are not a match. It will not be able to give me that, okay, this is the image or the face of Dhruba." A pure distance metric has zero invariance to transformations a human ignores instinctively.

Why This Matters — 20% was a big deal in its era

Running Prof's demo notebook (CIFAR-10, raw-pixel K-NN, no SIFT/HoG preprocessing) gives roughly 20% accuracy — it fails 80% of the time. Prof's point: at the time this was still meaningful. "If I'm even able to reduce the accidents by 20%, that's a huge KPI win" in a shop-floor safety-monitoring setting. K-NN was the first real attempt at automated image classification, not a good one by modern standards — but a necessary stepping stone.

# Prof's live demo, PyTorch + CIFAR-10 (torchvision)
transform = transforms.Compose([transforms.ToTensor(), transforms.Normalize(...)])
train_data, test_data = load_cifar10(transform)          # 50,000 train / 10,000 test
train_data, val_data = split(train_data, 40000, 10000)   # 90/10 split of the training set

def nearest_neighbor_predict(train_x, train_y, test_x):
    preds = []
    for t in test_x:
        dists = l1_distance(train_x, t)      # or l2_distance
        preds.append(train_y[argmin(dists)])
    return preds
# accuracy ≈ 0.20 — intensity-to-intensity matching only
Where this generalizes

Every distance-based retrieval system today inherits this exact weakness and this exact fix-in-progress. Prof's own aside: "the entire world is using distance-based methodology only for everything... say for instance, you are doing retrieval using LLMs, right? That is distance based only, right? How closely two chunks are." RAG's cosine-similarity chunk retrieval is K-NN with an embedding space swapped in for raw pixels — the embedding is what buys the invariance raw pixels never had.

05

Train / Validation / Test & Cross-Validation

Transcript

Why two-way train/test isn't enough

K-NN's hyperparameter is K. Setting K=1 collapses everything into effectively one decision per point — "that does not work out good enough for you." Prof asked the class directly what goes wrong with a plain train/test split, and Patil's answer was accepted as correct: "we tweak parameters as per the test data, which ultimately biases the model towards the test data and not unseen data."

Prof's teaching analogy: "I teach you the table of two... say for instance, one of you says 2 × 2 is 5. It is incorrect, but I do not pay heed to it and you directly go to the exams and make a mistake — that's a concept of train and test, there is no real-time correction." The validation set is that real-time correction — it's used during training to tune hyperparameters, exactly like a teacher correcting a mistake before the exam.

Three-way split — what each part is actually for
Train → fit the model's parameters Validation → tune hyperparameters (e.g. K in K-NN) — "correction," done during training Test → measure generalization only, touched once, at the very end
Why cross-validation specifically (live Q&A, several students)

Raghavendra's answer, which Prof endorsed: cross-validation exists so the model is exposed to all the population variation the data represents, not just one arbitrary split. Prof's own example: facial data spans Asian, African, Caucasian, Hispanic — and within India, North/South/East/West Indian — and a single train/test split can accidentally put all of one group only in test. Folds are random subsamples of the full population, each with its own class balance (e.g. one fold 5 true/3 false, another 3 true/5 false) — training across folds forces the model to learn variance robustly rather than overfitting to one lucky (or biased) split.

Choosing the number of folds

Sweep fold count (1, 2, 3, 5, 6, ...) and track cross-validation accuracy (averaged across folds at each fold-count). Accuracy rises, peaks, then drops. The fold count at the peak is your optimal K-fold value — Prof's illustrative number was 7, but stressed it's dataset-dependent ("in your data's case, it might be 4; in some other case, three").

Key Insight — the three-way split, restated for the exam

"Train is to train the model, validation is to tune the hyperparameters, and test is to understand the generalizability of the model" — is it applicable in India, Europe, China, the Americas? That's literally the question test-set performance answers. Hyperparameter tuning happens on validation, never on test — using test to tune anything reintroduces exactly the bias the three-way split exists to prevent.

Research Enhanced

Modern deep learning has largely moved away from k-fold CV when datasets are large. At ImageNet scale, a single held-out validation split is standard — the variance in performance estimates across different splits shrinks as sample size grows, so k-fold's variance reduction rarely justifies training k separate models at k× the compute (Raschka, "Model Evaluation, Model Selection, and Algorithm Selection in ML," 2018). The calculus reverses for small datasets, especially medical imaging: with only hundreds or low-thousands of scans, a single random split gives unreliable estimates, so k-fold (or repeated k-fold) is near-mandatory — the GaNDLF clinical deep-learning framework (Pati et al., 2021) builds it in by default for exactly this reason.

Data leakage — information from outside training improperly influencing it — has caused well-documented real failures. Zech et al., PLOS Medicine, 2018: CNNs trained to detect pneumonia across NIH, Mount Sinai, and Indiana hospital systems partly learned to recognize which hospital produced the scan (portable X-rays correlate with sicker patients), so accuracy dropped sharply on an unseen hospital's data. Winkler et al., JAMA Dermatology, 2019: a market-approved melanoma-detection CNN's accuracy was significantly skewed by surgical skin markings and rulers present in training images — it had partly learned to key off annotation artifacts, not the lesion.

Nested cross-validation (an outer loop for performance estimation wrapping an inner loop for hyperparameter tuning) is necessary only when you must both tune and report an unbiased estimate from the same limited dataset — reusing ordinary k-fold for both inflates reported accuracy (Cawley & Talbot, 2010). With a separate, sufficiently large test set, it's usually overkill.

06

Linear Classifiers

Transcript

From data-driven distance to a parametric approach

K-NN stores the entire dataset and compares at inference time — a data-driven, non-parametric approach. Linear classifiers instead learn a fixed, compact set of weights and biases that summarize the training set — a parametric approach. Once trained, prediction needs only one matrix multiply, not N comparisons — this directly fixes K-NN's inverted time-complexity problem.

The score function — CIFAR-10 dimensions, worked exactly as in lecture
Image: 32 × 32 × 3 (3 = RGB channels) Flatten: x ∈ ℝ^3072 (32×32×3 = 3072, a single 1×3072 row vector) Score function: f(x, W) = Wx + b — literally y = mx + c, generalized to matrices Weight matrix: W ∈ ℝ^(10 × 3072) — one 3072-length weight row per class, for CIFAR-10's 10 classes Bias vector: b ∈ ℝ^(10 × 1) Output: f(x,W) ∈ ℝ^(10 × 1) — one raw class score per class ("10×3072 · 3072×1 → 10×1")
Prof. Dhruba, connecting this to what you already write

"If you remember your deep learning final layers, right, what do you do before you put it to softmax or sigmoid? You resize all images to the same size... and then you flatten the entire image." Every "Flatten()" layer you've written in Keras/PyTorch is this exact step — the linear classifier isn't new machinery, it's the last layer of every network you've already built, in isolation.

Toy worked example (4-pixel image, 3 classes)

Prof worked a small numeric example: 3 classes (cat, dog, sheep), an image with only 4 pixels. Weight matrix is 3×4, multiplied by the 4×1 flattened pixel vector, plus a 3×1 bias, giving a 3×1 class-score vector. The class with the extreme (in his example, most negative, since he hadn't yet applied softmax) score is read off as the winner — "for a given weight vector" this is before any loss function has been applied; it's purely the raw score.

Limitation flagged live

Linear classifiers can't bend a decision boundary

  • A linear classifier draws one hyperplane per class — it has no way to carve out a non-linear region (Prof's example: concentric circles alternating positive/negative labels — "there's no way you can draw and classify it using a single decision boundary").
  • Asked what fixes this, students answered SVM with a kernel function (correct) — but Prof noted SVMs only scale to small datasets (~10k–50k) because kernel optimization needs many hyperparameters.
  • The actual fix, teed up for Session 10: pass Wx+b through a non-linear activation function before the next layer — this is precisely what turns a stack of linear classifiers into a neural network capable of curved/folded decision boundaries.
Research Enhanced

Visualizing the rows of W as images is one of the clearest diagnostics for what a linear classifier actually learned — Stanford's CS231n Linear Classification notes (Karpathy et al.) give the canonical CIFAR-10 example. The learned "ship" template comes out saturated blue (ships are usually photographed against ocean/sky); "car" comes out reddish (red cars are overrepresented in the training set); most tellingly, "horse" looks like a two-headed horse, because the dataset has horses facing both directions and a linear classifier has only one row per class — it can't represent both poses, so it blurs them into one composite. That's the template-matching view's core weakness: all intra-class appearance variation collapses into a single static prototype per class.

This rigidity is exactly why one linear classifier can't solve XOR: its positive/negative classes aren't linearly separable — no single hyperplane can split four XOR points correctly, the same failure mode as concentric circles. Minsky & Papert, "Perceptrons," 1969 proved this formally for single-layer perceptrons, and the result is widely credited with redirecting funding away from neural networks and contributing to the first AI winter — until backpropagation and multilayer networks (Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams, 1986) revived the field by showing hidden layers overcome exactly this limitation.

Linear classifiers remain in production use today, chiefly as a lightweight final layer on frozen deep features. "Linear probing" — training logistic regression on frozen CNN/ViT embeddings — is the standard evaluation protocol for self-supervised representation learning (SimCLR, MoCo, DINO), since linear-probe accuracy correlates strongly with downstream transfer quality.

07

Loss Functions

Transcript

What a loss function is actually doing

Ground truth is a one-hot vector — for a cat image, "cat" = 1, every other class = 0. The loss function measures how far the predicted score vector is from that one-hot target, averaged over the whole dataset, and that average is what gets minimized via backpropagation + an optimizer (SGD in Prof's demo).

Loss functions covered — binary vs multi-class vs margin
Sigmoid (binary): σ(z) = 1 / (1 + e^(−z)) → paired with binary cross-entropy for cat/not-cat style problems Softmax (multi-class): P(class i) = e^(zᵢ) / Σⱼ e^(zⱼ) — exponentiate each class's score, divide by the sum over all classes → paired with cross-entropy loss — the loss used for CIFAR-10's 10-way problem SVM / max-margin: maximize the distance (Δ, the margin) from the hyperplane to the nearest point of either class

Why margin matters — the two-zero-loss-hyperplanes problem

Prof's whiteboard example: two candidate decision boundaries can both achieve zero classification loss (every point correctly separated), yet one keeps every point comfortably far from the boundary while the other barely squeaks by. "A good loss function should not give me ambiguity between multiple decision points" — of the two zero-loss lines, the one with the larger margin generalizes better. This is exactly what SVM optimizes for, and why it "works better" than an arbitrary zero-loss hyperplane picked by chance.

Why This Matters — picking a loss isn't cosmetic

Binary problem → sigmoid + binary cross-entropy. Multi-class → softmax + cross-entropy (today's CIFAR-10 case). Want maximum-margin separation instead of just any zero-loss boundary → SVM/hinge loss. Prof: "I can go with max margin, I can go with cross entropy, I can go with anything else... but cross entropy is the most popular one of these." Once you have a differentiable loss, training is the same recipe every time: forward pass → compute loss → backprop → optimizer step → repeat.

Research Enhanced

Softmax cross-entropy and multiclass SVM hinge loss (Weston-Watkins or Crammer-Singer formulations) penalize scores differently. SVM loss charges max(0, s_j − s_yi + margin) per wrong class — once the correct class beats every wrong class by the margin, loss for that example is exactly zero; it "does not micromanage" scores beyond that (Karpathy, CS231n notes). Softmax cross-entropy minimizes −log(p_yi), which only reaches zero as p_yi→1 — it never stops pushing probability toward the correct class, even on already-correct examples. This is a large part of why modern deep learning almost universally uses softmax cross-entropy: its smooth, everywhere-nonzero gradients suit SGD better than hinge loss's flat, piecewise-linear landscape, and its calibrated probabilities are useful outputs in their own right (Lapin et al., CVPR 2016).

A common refinement on top of softmax cross-entropy is label smoothing: soften the one-hot target to a mixture with the uniform distribution (typically ε=0.1), introduced because one-hot targets drive networks toward increasingly extreme, overconfident logits that hurt generalization and calibration (Szegedy et al., "Rethinking the Inception Architecture," CVPR 2016).

Before 2012, the dominant pipeline paired hand-crafted features (SIFT, HoG, Fisher Vectors) with a linear/hinge-loss SVM — the ILSVRC-2012 runner-up used exactly this, at ~26.2% top-5 error. AlexNet, trained end-to-end with softmax cross-entropy rather than a separately-trained SVM on fixed features, cut that to ~15.3% — the loss-function choice and end-to-end learning, not just depth, is a key reason softmax cross-entropy displaced SVM+hand-crafted-feature pipelines as the field's default.

Prof also tried visualizing K-NN's decision boundaries across different cross-validation folds; K-NN's accuracy swung between 0.14 and 0.25 depending on the fold, while the linear classifier stayed stable around 0.40 across populations. Towards CNNs — Session 10 Preview

Preview
Explicitly deferred by Prof to Session 10

"We will start with the actual CNNs... we'll see how to replace both of these technologies [K-NN and linear classifiers], and we'll be moving towards Transformers and towards Vision Transformers, which are the state of art today." Prof also flagged that Transformers get comparatively less classroom weight because they're "relatively yet untested within the imaging world" for many production tasks — YOLO (detection) and U-Net (segmentation) remain the dominant non-Transformer architectures in practice, even though Transformers dominate the rest of deep learning.

Research Enhanced — LeNet vs. AlexNet

LeNet-5 (LeCun, Bottou, Bengio & Haffner, 1998) was built for practical, narrow-domain optical character recognition — not general image classification. It powered handwritten digit recognition for postal codes and bank-check reading systems, reportedly processing millions of checks per day in the late 1990s. Its significance is architectural, not empirical at scale: it introduced the now-standard CNN template of alternating convolution and pooling layers, but was trained on small, clean, low-resolution grayscale data (MNIST-scale), not natural images. CNNs didn't become mainstream for large-scale image classification until AlexNet (Krizhevsky, Sutskever & Hinton, 2012) won ILSVRC-2012 at 15.3% top-5 error versus 26.2% for the runner-up — roughly 14 years after LeNet-5.

Reality check — this framing is now outdated

Prof's framing that ViT is "relatively untested" for imaging was accurate in earlier years but should be flagged as outdated by 2026. Vision Transformer (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020, "An Image is Worth 16x16 Words") showed transformers match or beat CNNs on classification given enough pretraining data, and by 2024–2025 ViT-based and hybrid architectures are competitive with or ahead of pure CNNs on most classification leaderboards (recent ViT variants report ~86% top-1 on ImageNet).

More importantly, the two domains Prof named as CNN's territory — detection and segmentation — are exactly where ViTs have become dominant. Segment Anything (SAM, Meta, 2023) and SAM 2 are ViT-based foundation models and are now the de facto standard for promptable, general-purpose segmentation, effectively superseding classic U-Net-style approaches for many tasks. DINOv2 (Meta, 2023) is a ViT backbone trained via self-supervised distillation on 142M images, now used off-the-shelf across classification, segmentation, and depth estimation. Even in detection, transformer-hybrid variants (ViT-YOLO) report competitive or superior mAP versus pure-CNN YOLO on COCO/VOC — though pure-CNN YOLO retains an efficiency edge for real-time, low-latency edge deployment. Net: CNNs keep the advantage for cheap real-time inference, but "untested" no longer describes ViTs — they're now the backbone of the leading segmentation and self-supervised vision foundation models, and are competitive-to-SOTA in classification and detection too.

What bridges Session 9 → Session 10

The single sentence to hold onto: a linear classifier is Wx + b; a neural network layer is activation(Wx + b). Everything else Session 9 built — the flattening, the weight/bias shapes, the softmax + cross-entropy loss, the SGD training loop — carries over unchanged into CNNs. What changes is (a) the non-linear activation after each linear layer, and (b) convolution replacing full flattening so spatial structure isn't thrown away before the first layer even sees it.

After Session 10 (CNNs + Vision Transformers), the course proceeds to segmentation, then detection, then tracking — mirroring the "what → where → track" three-stage framing Prof opened this session with.
