Module 3 · Session 1

Data Architecture — Definitions & Why

Understanding what data architecture means, why it matters, and the foundational frameworks that define it.

📘 Definition · TOGAF
TOGAF (The Open Group Architectural Framework): A description of the structure and interaction of the enterprise's major types and sources of data, logical data assets, physical data assets, and data management resources.

Think of TOGAF as the architectural blueprint — it answers what data exists and how it connects.
📘 Definition · DAMA-DMBOK
DAMA–DMBOK (Data Management Association, DM Body of Knowledge): Identifying the data needs of the enterprise (regardless of structure) and designing/maintaining master blueprints to meet those needs. Using these blueprints to guide data integration, control data assets, and align data investments with business strategy.
📘 Core Definitions
Data Architecture — The design of systems to support the evolving data needs of an enterprise, achieved by flexible and reversible decisions reached through a careful evaluation of trade-offs.

Data Engineering Architecture — The systems and frameworks that make up the key sections of the data engineering lifecycle.
🎯 Exam Tip
The phrase "flexible and reversible decisions" is key in the Data Architecture definition. Exam questions may test whether you understand that good architecture allows course-correction — it's not set-in-stone. TOGAF = structure/sources. DAMA = needs/blueprints/business alignment.

Why Data Architecture Matters

⚡ Rapid Change
Data requirements change rapidly. New tools and practices arrive on a near-daily basis, creating the need for understanding good architecture to keep pace.
🗺️ Blueprint for Data
Translates business requirements into design — new ways to capture and serve data, knowing trade-offs with patterns, technologies, and tools.
🏗️ Foundation for Engineering
Successful data engineering is built upon rock-solid data architecture. Without it, systems become fragile, expensive, and hard to maintain.
📐 Aligns Strategy
Reflects current and future state of data systems, supporting the organization's long-term data needs and business strategy.
🔬 Research Addition · McKinsey (2024)
McKinsey's research shows companies with well-defined data architectures achieve up to 40% faster time-to-insight compared to ad-hoc data management. Modern "data products" thinking (2023–2024) extends data architecture to treat data assets as first-class products with their own lifecycle — architecture as the foundation of data product development.

Source: McKinsey Digital, "Revisiting Data Architecture for Next-Gen Data Products," 2024
💡 Instructor Insight (Lecture)
Prof. DVS mentioned the aviation example: a single Boeing 737 generates 20 terabytes of engine data per hour. Multiply across the US fleet — the scale is staggering. His point: "Everywhere, data is underestimated; later, they struggle." Good architecture prevents this. Also note: 300,000 sensor parameters per aircraft!

Categorization · By Centralization

Centralized, Decentralized & Hybrid

Data architectures differ in how much control is concentrated in one place versus distributed across business units.

📘 Centralized Architecture
Provides a single point of control for data governance, auditing, and reporting. Uses authoritative sources, a single intelligent landing area, and a data aggregation layer across all business units.
✅ Advantages
Consistent governance, single source of truth, easier compliance, simplified enterprise reporting, reduced duplication.
⚠️ Limitations
Single point of failure, bottlenecks for domain-specific needs, slower to adapt to new use cases, can stifle agility.
💡 Real-World Example
Banking and healthcare organizations operating in highly regulated environments (RBI, HIPAA). A central data warehouse holds all transaction/patient records, with strict access controls — perfect for audit trails.
📘 Decentralized Architecture
Data is organized and optimized front-to-back within business unit silos, each with its own sources, aggregation, and reporting. Platforms aggregate across units for enterprise-level reporting only.
💡 Real-World Example
Insurance companies where MDM (Master Data Management) and business domain data are decentralized to accommodate different customer bases, core systems, and data products across lines of business (auto, health, life).
⚠️ Common Pitfall
Can lead to data silos — duplicated data, inconsistent definitions, and difficulty getting a unified enterprise view. Without governance policies, decentralized architectures become ungovernable.
📘 Hybrid Architecture
Data and platforms are organized by data domain with single golden sources and no duplication across data domains. Combines centralized MDM with federated storage for individual domains.
💡 Real-World Example
Telecommunications companies like Reliance Jio: centralized master data for subscribers and billing, but federated storage for network performance data (differs by region/tower).
🔬 Research · Gartner 2024
Gartner reports that 72% of large enterprises now operate hybrid data architectures, combining centralized governance with domain-specific storage — making it the most common pattern in production today.
Source: Gartner, "Data Architecture Trends," 2024

Categorization · By Storage

Data Warehouse, Data Lake & Lakehouse

The three major storage paradigms — each optimized for different data types, velocities, and use cases.

📘 Data Warehouse
A structured, subject-oriented, integrated, time-variant, non-volatile collection of data supporting management's decision-making. Uses ETL (Extract-Transform-Load) processes — data is transformed BEFORE loading.

✅ Best For
Structured data, business analytics, fast querying on known schema, compliance reporting, BI dashboards.
⚠️ Limitations
Can't handle unstructured data. Schema must be defined upfront. Expensive to scale. Rigid ETL pipelines.
🔬 Research · Industry Tools (2024)
Leading platforms: Snowflake, Google BigQuery, Amazon Redshift, Azure Synapse. Snowflake's "Data Cloud" has dominated since 2020, offering cloud-native DW with separation of compute and storage. BigQuery offers serverless DW with ML integration via BigQuery ML.
Source: Gartner Magic Quadrant for Cloud Database Management Systems, 2024
📘 Data Lake
A centralized repository that stores all structured, semi-structured, and unstructured data at any scale. Uses ELT (Extract-Load-Transform) — data loaded raw, transformed later. Schema-on-read approach.
⚠️ Data Lake Failure Modes
Data Swamp — Disorganized, unstructured storage no one can navigate.
Dark Data — Data collected but never used for any purpose.
WORN — Write Once, Read Never. Prof. DVS: "Facebook and Netflix still have lots of WORN data."
Grows unmanageable — Without governance, lakes become costly black holes.
🔬 Research · Real Usage (2024)
AWS S3 + Apache Spark is the dominant Data Lake stack. Meta (Facebook) uses a lake with 500+ petabytes. Netflix uses Iceberg (table format) on S3 for their lake. According to IDC 2024, organizations waste ~$3.1M annually on unused dark data storage.
Source: IDC Data Sphere Report, 2024
📘 Data Lakehouse
A convergence of Data Warehouse and Data Lake. Introduced by Databricks. Incorporates DW-style governance, controls, and ACID transactions while storing data in object storage (like a lake) and supporting variety of query and transformation engines.

ACID = Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, Durability.
💡 Key Insight
The Lakehouse solves the classic dilemma: lakes are cheap and flexible but ungoverned; warehouses are governed but rigid and expensive. The Lakehouse adds a metadata and governance layer on top of the lake (e.g., Delta Lake format adds ACID transactions to S3/ADLS).
🔬 Research · Table Formats (2024)
Three competing open table formats power lakehouses today: Delta Lake (Databricks), Apache Iceberg (Netflix, now dominant in cloud), and Apache Hudi (Uber). As of 2024, Iceberg is winning cloud adoption — AWS, Google, and Azure all offer native Iceberg support.
Source: Databricks, Netflix Engineering Blog, 2024
📘 Data Mart
A more refined subset of a warehouse designed to serve analytics and reporting for a single sub-organization, department, or line of business. Every department gets its own data mart, specific to its needs.
💡 Lecture Example
HR Mart — tracks recruitment performance metrics.
IT Mart — dashboards on ticket frequency by type.

Running analytics on the full DW hurts performance. Data marts pre-aggregate the relevant subset → significantly faster queries.
🎯 Exam Tip
Data Mart = special case of Data Warehouse. Key differentiator: scope (department vs. enterprise). Data Marts provide an additional stage of transformation beyond initial ETL/ELT pipelines — they pre-join and aggregate for performance.

Data Warehouse · Schema Design

Star Schema vs Snowflake Schema

Data warehouse schemas define how facts and dimensions are organized for optimal analytical query performance.

📘 Data Warehouse Schema
A logical structure or blueprint that organizes how data is stored, related, and queried within a data warehouse. Defines tables, keys, and relationships among facts (quantitative/measurable data) and dimensions (context data like product, time, location).
📘 Star Schema
At the center sits a fact table (measurable data like sales, revenue). Surrounding it are dimension tables (product names, customer info, dates) that connect directly to the fact table — forming a star shape.
Star Schema Structure
DIM: Date
DIM: Customer
↕ connects
FACT: Sales
↕ connects
DIM: Product
DIM: Store
✅ Advantages
Faster queries — Fewer joins needed. Denormalized design stores related data together. Easy to understand — even for non-technical business users. Common in BI tools (Tableau, Power BI).
⚠️ Drawbacks
Data redundancy — Due to denormalization, dimension tables repeat information → increases storage. E.g., "Electronics > Laptops" stored separately for every laptop product row.
📘 Snowflake Schema
An extension of the star schema where dimension tables are split into smaller sub-dimensions — normalized to remove redundancy. Like snowflakes branching from a central fact table.
✅ Advantages
Less data redundancy — normalization prevents storing the same data multiple times. Efficient storage for large datasets. Better for complex hierarchical data (e.g., City → State → Country).
⚠️ Drawbacks
Slower queries — more joins between sub-dimension tables. More complex to design and maintain. Harder for business users to understand without training.

Kimball Method✅ Primary recommendationUsed when storage is critical
🎯 Key Distinction for Exams
Star = Fast queries, redundant storage, simple. Snowflake = Slower queries, efficient storage, complex.
The trade-off: query speed vs. storage efficiency. Ralph Kimball (Data Warehouse Toolkit) strongly advocates for Star Schema for most BI use cases.
Categorization · By Governance

Data Mesh & Data Fabric

Modern governance architectures that address the limitations of monolithic, centralized data platforms.

📘 Data Mesh — Zhamak Dehghani
A sociotechnical approach that decentralizes data ownership. Instead of flowing data from domains into a centrally owned lake or platform, each domain hosts and serves its own domain datasets in an easily consumable way.

Four Key Principles

1️⃣ Domain-Oriented Ownership
Data ownership belongs to the business domain that generates it — not a central team. The sales team owns sales data; the logistics team owns shipping data.
2️⃣ Data as a Product
Data is treated like a product with discoverability, documentation, SLAs, versioning, and quality standards — not just a byproduct of operational systems.
3️⃣ Self-Serve Infrastructure
A platform team provides centralized infrastructure tools (data catalogs, monitoring) so domain teams can build pipelines without deep data engineering expertise.
4️⃣ Federated Governance
Cross-domain policies (compliance, security, interoperability) are set centrally but enforced locally — balancing autonomy with enterprise standards.
💡 Real-World Example
An e-commerce company: the Order domain exposes an order_events data product with schema, SLAs, and usage guidelines. The Customer domain exposes a customer_360 product. Each is owned, maintained, and documented by its respective team — no central data team bottleneck.
🔬 Research · Adoption (2024)
Companies like JPMorgan Chase, Zalando, and HelloFresh have publicly documented Data Mesh implementations. As of 2024, ~35% of Fortune 500 companies are either piloting or running Data Mesh architectures. However, Gartner warns it is complex to implement without strong data culture.
Source: Gartner Hype Cycle for Data Management, 2024
📘 Data Fabric
Creates a unified data layer that integrates information from lakes, warehouses, databases, and SaaS tools through APIs, change data capture (CDC), and virtualization technologies. Data stays where it lives — the fabric provides unified access.

Key Characteristics

🔗 Unified Data Access
Single logical access layer over multiple physical stores — users don't need to know where data lives.
⚡ Seamless Integration
Orchestrates data pipelines across heterogeneous sources — on-premise, multi-cloud, SaaS.
🔒 Security & Compliance
Governance and compliance policies enforced uniformly across all connected sources.
📊 Real-Time Insights
Supports streaming and batch, enabling real-time analytics without moving all data to one place.
💡 Classic Example
360° Customer View: Combines sales data from Salesforce CRM + transaction logs from a data lake + social media sentiment via APIs — all through the Data Fabric without physically moving or duplicating data.

Big Data Systems

Big Data — The 5 Vs

Big data is characterized by its five dimensions, each creating unique challenges for architecture and processing.

📘 Big Data Definition
Big data refers to massive, complex data sets that traditional data management systems cannot handle. When properly collected, managed, and analyzed, it helps organizations discover new insights and make better business decisions.
💡 Lecture Example (Boeing 737)
Sensors on one aircraft cover more than 300,000 parameters. A Boeing 737 creates 20 terabytes of engine data per hour. A 6-hour NYC-to-LA flight = 240 TB per engine. Multiply by the US fleet — this is why data architecture is not optional.

The 5 Vs of Big Data

V1 Volume — Scale of Data
▼
The massive amount of data generated today — from web apps, IoT devices, transaction records, and more. Traditional storage and processing systems often struggle to handle it at scale. Scale: terabytes → petabytes → exabytes.
🔬 Scale Context (2024)
Google processes 8.5 billion searches/day. Meta generates ~100 petabytes of data daily. The global datasphere is projected to reach 175 zettabytes by 2025 (IDC, 2024).
V2 Velocity — Speed of Data
▼
The speed at which data flows into a system. Data arrives faster than ever — from real-time social media updates to high-frequency stock trading (microseconds). Requires stream processing architectures.
💡 Example
Twitter/X processes 6,000 tweets per second. Stock exchanges handle 1 million transactions per second. IoT sensors in smart factories emit data at millisecond intervals.
V3 Variety — Types of Data
▼
The many different formats big data takes — structured (tables), semi-structured (JSON, XML), and unstructured (images, video, text, audio). Traditional relational databases handle structured data only.
Structured CSV, SQL tables, Excel
Semi-structured JSON, XML, Parquet, Avro
Unstructured Images, video, PDFs, social media text
V4 Veracity — Quality of Data
▼
The accuracy and reliability of data. Big data comes in great quantities from varied sources — it can contain noise, errors, inconsistencies, and missing values. Poor veracity leads to poor decision-making (GIGO: Garbage In, Garbage Out).
🎯 Exam Connection
Veracity is why the lecture mentioned Data Quality Checks and Data Preprocessing as hands-on practicals (Google Colab Exercise 1).
V5 Value — Business Impact
▼
The real-world benefits organizations derive from big data — from optimizing operations to identifying marketing opportunities. Data without value is just cost. Architecture must enable value extraction.
🔬 Business Value (2024)
McKinsey reports companies that effectively use big data analytics outperform peers by 5-6% in productivity and profitability. Netflix estimates data-driven recommendations save $1 billion annually in customer retention.

Processing Architectures · Lambda

Lambda Architecture

The classic dual-path architecture combining batch and stream processing for scalable, fault-tolerant big data pipelines.

💡 Motivating Scenario (Lecture)
A banking institute builds a fraud analysis and alert system. They need:
(1) Batch analytics — identify fraud patterns, generate reports (can tolerate some delay)
(2) Real-time alerts — alert staff immediately when a suspicious transaction occurs
→ Same data must flow through both batch and stream processing. Lambda Architecture is the solution.
📘 Lambda Architecture
An architecture pattern that processes data through two independent paths — a batch layer (cold path) for high-accuracy historical processing and a speed layer (hot path) for low-latency real-time processing. Results from both are merged at the serving layer.

📘 Batch Layer (Cold Path)
Stores all incoming data in its raw, immutable form and performs batch processing. Results stored as batch views. High accuracy but higher latency.
✅ Properties
Immutable raw data. High accuracy. Processes full historical dataset. Creates precomputed aggregated views.
⚡ Trade-off
Not subject to low-latency requirements. Can take hours to process. Results may be hours old when queried.
🔬 Tools Used (2024)
Apache Spark (dominant), Apache Hadoop/MapReduce (legacy), AWS Batch, Google Cloud Dataflow. Batch jobs typically run on schedules (nightly, hourly).
📘 Speed Layer (Hot Path)
Analyzes data in real time with lowest possible latency. Usually stored in NoSQL databases. Designed for speed at the expense of some accuracy. Updates serving layer with incremental updates.
✅ Properties
Ultra-low latency. Incrementally updates views. Changes stored as time-stamped events. Feeds real-time dashboards and alerts.
⚡ Trade-off
May introduce inaccuracy. Limited to recent data window. Higher operational complexity. NoSQL may have limited query capability.
🔬 Tools (2024)
Apache Kafka + Flink/Spark Streaming for ingestion/processing. Apache Cassandra, Redis, or DynamoDB as NoSQL speed layer store. Latency typically sub-second.
📘 Serving Layer
Provides a combined view by merging batch views (accurate, historical) with speed views (recent, timely). Can be implemented as a unified layer (Option 1) or separate layers (Option 2).
🎯 Two Options — Know the Difference!
Option 1: Unified Serving Layer — One layer merges batch + speed views on query. Query time merge.

Option 2: Separate Serving Layers — Speed View (for real-time queries), Batch View (for historical), Mixed Query (combines both at application level).

Exam questions may give a scenario and ask which option is in use.
🤖 Lambda for ML Workflows
Batch layer → Train models on comprehensive historical datasets (full data, high quality). Uses Fabric Data Science / Spark MLlib.

Speed layer → Real-time model inference and scoring on incoming data streams.

This dual approach = models trained on complete data + immediate predictions on new data. Perfect for fraud detection, recommendation engines, and predictive maintenance.
⚠️ Lambda's Key Challenge
Managing two separate codebases for the same logic (batch in Spark, stream in Flink/Kafka Streams) is the fundamental challenge. The same business logic written twice → inconsistency, bugs, and maintenance nightmare. This is exactly why Kappa Architecture was proposed.
🎯 Exam Scenario
"A company needs both historical pattern analysis AND real-time fraud alerts from the same transaction data." → Lambda Architecture.

"A live cricket match needs to be broadcast with highlights later." → Prof. DVS example: Real match = stream processing (hot path), Highlights reel = batch processing (cold path) = Lambda.
Processing Architectures · Kappa

Kappa Architecture

A stream-only alternative to Lambda, simplifying big data pipelines by processing everything through a single unified stream processing system.

📘 Kappa Architecture — Jay Kreps (LinkedIn)
Proposed by Jay Kreps (co-creator of Apache Kafka) as a response to Lambda's complexity. The core idea: use a single stream processing platform as the backbone for ALL data handling — ingestion, storage, and serving.

📘 Key Properties
  • Event data is immutable and all of it is collected (not a subset)
  • Data ingested as a stream into a distributed, fault-tolerant unified log (Apache Kafka)
  • Events are ordered; state changes only when a new event is appended
  • All event processing performed on the input stream, persisted as a real-time view
  • For batch recompute: replay the stream (no separate batch layer needed)
  • Replay uses parallelism to complete computation quickly
💡 Stream Replay = Batch Processing
In Kappa, when you need to reprocess historical data (equivalent to Lambda's batch layer), you simply replay stored events from the log from the beginning. Kafka retains events indefinitely (configurable). This eliminates the need for a separate batch system — the same code processes both real-time and historical data.
🔬 The Unified Log Pattern (2024)
Apache Kafka has become the de facto "unified log" for Kappa architectures. Confluent Platform (enterprise Kafka) is used by 70%+ of Fortune 500 companies. Modern tools like Apache Flink and Apache Spark Structured Streaming enable unified batch+stream processing on Kafka data.
Source: Confluent State of Data Streaming, 2024
⚠️ Three Real Challenges
1. Streaming complexity — "Easy to talk, harder to execute." Stateful stream processing, watermarks, late-arriving data, and exactly-once semantics are genuinely hard engineering problems.

2. Cost at scale — Turns out to be complicated and expensive in practice. Keeping large event logs in Kafka is storage-intensive. Stream processing clusters run 24/7 → higher operational cost than batch.

3. Historical data efficiency — Batch storage and processing remain much more efficient and cost-effective for enormous historical datasets. Replaying 5 years of events is very slow.
🎯 Exam Insight
Know that Kappa was proposed to solve Lambda's dual-codebase problem — but it introduced its own challenges. Neither is universally "better" — the choice depends on: data volume, latency requirements, team capabilities, and cost constraints.
🤖 Kappa for ML
Kappa enables unified ML workflows — same pipeline for training (stream replay) and inference (live stream). This simplifies model deployment since the same processing logic applies to both historical and real-time data.

Supports online learning algorithms that update models incrementally as new data arrives — continuous learning without full retraining cycles.
🔬 Real-World Use (2024)
Uber uses Kappa-inspired architecture for surge pricing ML models (real-time demand signals). LinkedIn uses it for feed ranking (Jay Kreps' original use case). TikTok uses streaming for real-time recommendation updates (sub-100ms).
Source: Engineering blogs, Uber, LinkedIn, 2024

🎯 Scenario Decision Guide
Use Lambda when: You need both high-accuracy historical analysis AND real-time results. Can afford operational overhead of two systems. Example: Banking fraud (batch for pattern mining + streaming for instant alerts).

Use Kappa when: All use cases are stream-first. Want simpler, unified codebase. Example: Live sports broadcasting, real-time recommendations, IoT sensor monitoring.

Exam trick: A cricket match live broadcast → Kappa (stream-first). The highlights compilation later → could be Lambda (batch highlights + live stream). Prof. DVS asked this directly in the lecture!
🔬 Industry Trend (2024)
The 2024 trend is toward "streaming-first" architectures, with tools like Apache Flink maturing enough to handle batch workloads natively (unified batch+stream API). Apache Beam and Flink are blurring the Lambda/Kappa distinction — a new "unified processing" paradigm is emerging. Google's Dataflow (Apache Beam) is a leading example.
Source: Kai Waehner, "Kappa Architecture Mainstream Replacing Batch Lambda," 2024

Term / Abbrev.Full FormDefinition & Usage
TOGAFThe Open Group Architectural FrameworkFramework defining data architecture as structure/interaction of enterprise data types and management resources.

DLData LakeRepository storing all data types at any scale. Uses ELT and schema-on-read.
WORNWrite Once Read NeverFailure mode of Data Lakes where data is stored but never used. Prof. DVS mentioned Facebook/Netflix have WORN data.
ACIDAtomicity, Consistency, Isolation, DurabilityProperties ensuring reliable database transactions. Data Lakehouses (Delta Lake) add ACID to object storage.
MDMMaster Data ManagementProcesses for ensuring enterprise-wide consistency of key data entities (customers, products). Central in hybrid architectures.
CDCChange Data CaptureTechnique for identifying and capturing data changes at the source for streaming to downstream systems. Used in Data Fabric.
BIBusiness IntelligenceTechnologies and practices for analysis of business data. Data Warehouses are primary BI storage.
IoTInternet of ThingsNetwork of physical devices generating sensor data. A key big data source (velocity + volume).
NoSQLNot Only SQLNon-relational database used in Lambda's speed layer for low-latency writes/reads (Cassandra, Redis, DynamoDB).
HDFSHadoop Distributed File SystemDistributed storage system for big data. Core component of Hadoop ecosystem, stores data across clusters.
SLAService Level AgreementDefined contract for data quality, freshness, and availability. Required for data products in Data Mesh.
MapReduce—Distributed processing algorithm that splits (Map) and aggregates (Reduce) data in parallel across clusters. Core Hadoop programming model.
Lambda (arch)Lambda ArchitectureDual-path big data pattern with batch layer (cold) + speed layer (hot) + serving layer. Named after λ (two paths).
Kappa (arch)Kappa ArchitectureSingle-stream architecture using a unified log (Kafka) as backbone. Eliminates separate batch layer.
KafkaApache KafkaDistributed streaming platform used as "unified log" in Kappa architecture. Created at LinkedIn by Jay Kreps.
SparkApache SparkUnified analytics engine for large-scale data processing. Supports batch (RDD, DataFrames) and streaming (Structured Streaming).
FlinkApache FlinkStream processing framework with strong exactly-once guarantees and low latency. Increasingly popular over Spark for streaming.
Data Mesh—Governance architecture by Zhamak Dehghani. Decentralizes data ownership to domains, treats data as products.
Data Fabric—Technical architecture creating a unified virtual data access layer across multiple physical stores via APIs and virtualization.
Data Mart—Department-specific subset of a Data Warehouse. Focused analytics store for a single business unit (HR, IT, Sales).
LakehouseData LakehouseConvergence of DW + Data Lake. Databricks innovation adding ACID transactions and governance to object storage (Delta Lake).
Star Schema—Denormalized DW schema with central fact table surrounded by dimension tables. Faster queries, more storage.
Snowflake Schema—Normalized DW schema where dimension tables are split into sub-dimensions. Less storage, slower queries.
Fact Table—Central table in DW schema containing measurable data (sales amounts, transaction values). Has foreign keys to dimension tables.
Dimension Table—Context table in DW schema (Product, Customer, Date, Location). Describes "who, what, when, where" of facts.
Data Swamp—Failed Data Lake — disorganized, unstructured, and unusable. Caused by lack of governance and metadata.
Dark Data—Data collected and stored during operations but never used for insights or decisions. Represents pure cost.
5 VsFive Vs of Big DataVolume, Velocity, Variety, Veracity, Value — the five dimensions characterizing big data challenges.
