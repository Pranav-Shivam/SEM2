Principles of Data · Topic 1

Data as an Asset

Why data is valuable — and when it stops being worth the cost

📘 Definition
Data is an asset when it can be transformed into actionable insight, fuelling better decisions, improved models, and competitive advantage. Like any asset, its value must be weighed against the cost of acquiring, storing, and maintaining it.

The Model Error Rate vs. Data Volume Graph

This is one of the most examined graphs in this course. It plots two competing curves against data volume (log scale):

■ Blue — Model Error Rate (decreases) ■ Red — Likelihood of Data Problems (increases)
⚡ Frequently Tested
  • Blue line (error rate): Drops sharply as data grows → bigger data makes models smarter. After ~10⁸–10⁹ data points, adding more data gives negligible improvement.
  • Red line (data problems): Starts low, rises steeply at large volumes → noise, corruption, privacy breaches, GIGO.
  • Intersection point = Optimal data volume — beyond this, cost and risk outweigh accuracy gains.
  • The phrase "sweet spot" in the exam = the intersection point.

Netflix Case Study (Lecture Example)

🌐 Real-World Connection
Netflix is cited as the gold standard for a data-informed company. Their data use:
  • Personalization: Recommendations based on viewing history
  • Content Acquisition: Predicting whether original shows will be hits before they are produced (e.g., Kapil Sharma Show — 52 countries, comedy popularity analysis)
  • Streaming Quality: Optimizing video encoding and server distribution for smooth playback

Source: research.netflix.com

🔬 Research Addition — Industry Context (2024–2025)
IBM's Global CEO Study (2024) found 72% of top-performing CEOs believe competitive advantage depends on who has the most advanced generative AI capabilities. This directly implies: to leverage AI, organizations must first organize their data architecture. CEOs rank customer/employee experience, enhanced decision-making, and accelerated growth as top AI-driven priorities.
Source: IBM Institute for Business Value CEO Survey, 2024

Why "More Data = Better" is a Myth

⚠️ Common Misconception
Students often write "more data always improves the model." This is wrong beyond the optimal volume. After the saturation point, you get GIGO (Garbage In, Garbage Out), skyrocketing storage costs, increased privacy/breach risk, and harder governance. Always qualify your answer with "up to an optimal volume."

Data as a Liability

The dual nature of data — Collect, Store, Delete lifecycle challenges

📘 Definition — Dual Nature of Data
The same dataset can be an asset (value driver) or a liability (legal, financial, reputational burden) depending on how it is collected, stored, and managed. Like the proverb "one man's food is another man's poison."

Challenges at the Collect Phase

The biggest myth: "More data = better, and this stuff is easy." — Both are false.

📘 PII — Personally Identifiable Information
Any data that can uniquely identify a person (name, address, ID number, biometrics, etc.). Collecting PII requires explicit consent and a defined deletion policy by law (e.g., GDPR).

Restrictions in Data Collection

🌐 Real-World: Target Pregnancy Prediction (Lecture Example)
Target used purchase history to predict which customers were pregnant — so they could send baby product coupons. Their model was so accurate it identified a teenage girl as pregnant before she had told her family. When the father saw the coupons, it became a major privacy scandal. Lesson: Public shopping data becomes a liability when it uncovers deeply personal information.

3 Approaches to Handling Sensitive Data in Storage

#ApproachHowNote
1Access RestrictionRestrict external AND internal users; granular permissions; audit logsRestrict even employees — key exam phrase
2Anonymization / PseudonymizationReplace PII with fake identifiers (reversible); e.g., postcode for weatherReversible with additional key; protects from casual inspection
3 ✅Connection Removal (Recommended)Break the link between PII and model data entirelyNot trivially reversible; harder than it sounds but most secure
🔍 Pseudonymization Example — Postcode+

For weather predictions, you don't need the exact house address — a general area code (postcode zone) is sufficient. Replace the precise postcode with a zone-level code. This is a form of differential privacy — you preserve the utility (weather correlation) without the liability (knowing exactly where someone lives). The replacement is reversible if you have the mapping key.

⚡ Exam Trap
Question: "Which approach to PII in the Store phase is recommended?" → Answer: Connection Removal (Approach 3). Not access restriction, not pseudonymization. The keyword is "recommended."

When Should Data Be Deleted?

  • When the user specifies a time period
  • When the organization no longer needs it
  • When governing policies set a cutoff (e.g., GDPR Article 5.1(f))

Lecture Example: Job portals retain your profile for 6 months only. After that, you must recreate it.

📘 GDPR — General Data Protection Regulation
European Union law governing personal data collection, processing, and deletion. Sets legal obligations for when and how data must be erased. Article 17 is the "Right to Erasure" (right to be forgotten).

Why Deletion is Harder Than It Seems

⚠️ Warning
Shift+Delete ≠ Gone. Recovery software can restore files. More critically:
  • Data may have been written to tape drives for archival
  • Data may exist in multiple backup copies
  • Data may be replicated across geographic servers
  • Even after primary system deletion, archived copies survive
🔬 Research Addition — GDPR Enforcement Reality (2024)
In 2023–24, GDPR fines exceeded €2.1 billion, with Meta alone fined €1.2 billion for improper data transfers. The "right to erasure" under GDPR Article 17 is extremely difficult to enforce in practice when data has been replicated to cloud CDNs, third-party analytics, and backup tapes. This is exactly the "harder than it seems" problem the lecture describes.
Source: CNIL, DPA enforcement tracker, 2024

Medical Records — The Classic Example

Medical records can be both an asset (enables treatment, research) and a liability (HIPAA violations, breach lawsuits, reputational damage). The same data item flips roles based on who holds it and how securely.

Principles of Data · Topic 3

Data Sensitivity of ML Pipelines

Why ML pipelines are more sensitive to data issues than regular data pipelines

📘 Definition
ML pipelines are unusually sensitive because models memorize and propagate their training data — leakage, bias, corruption, and distribution shifts all flow downstream into predictions.

The Lecture Scenario (E-commerce Example)

🌐 Lecture Scenario
An e-commerce company wants to predict New Year's Eve sales (Dec 31) across all countries/regions/languages. Due to a technical or human error, data from one region is lost. The data pipeline keeps running normally, but the ML pipeline behaves very differently. Why?

The Answer

Pipeline TypeEffect of Missing Region DataWhy
Data Pipeline✅ Continues normallyNot predicting anything — it just moves data. Missing region = smaller dataset, pipeline still runs.
ML Pipeline❌ Behaves abnormallyModel includes predictions — missing data = distribution shift → wrong predictions → wrong sales forecasts → business impact.

Sensitivity Comparison

Normal Data Pipelines — Sensitive to:
  • Volume of input data
  • Correctness of input data
ML Pipelines — Sensitive to:
  • Volume of input data
  • Correctness of input data
  • Changes to distribution of data ← extra!
⚡ Frequently Tested
The key differentiator for ML pipelines vs. normal pipelines is sensitivity to distribution shift. Both care about volume and correctness, but only ML pipelines are broken by a change in data distribution — even if the total volume and format are fine.

Training–Serving Skew Research Addition

🔬 Research — Training–Serving Skew (2024 Industry Practice)
A model can score 94% accuracy in offline tests yet fail catastrophically in production because a feature is stale, missing, or computed differently at serving time than at training time. This is called training–serving skew. Google's ML best practices manual describes it as "one of the most common and hardest-to-debug issues in production ML." Solutions include feature stores (Feast, Tecton) that guarantee identical feature computation in both training and serving.
Source: Google ML Engineering best practices; Tecton feature store docs, 2024

Data Drift & Concept Drift Research Addition

🔬 Research Addition
  • Data Drift: The statistical distribution of input features changes over time (e.g., consumer behavior shifts post-pandemic). Models trained on old distributions silently degrade.
  • Concept Drift: The relationship between features and the target label changes (e.g., fraud patterns evolve to evade detection). More dangerous — the data looks fine but ground truth has shifted.
Modern MLOps platforms (MLflow, Evidently AI, Arize) provide automated drift monitoring dashboards.
Source: Evidently AI documentation, 2024–2025

Data Reliability

The 5 characteristics of reliable data in distributed environments

📘 Definition
Data Reliability is the set of minimum characteristics expected from data in a growing distributed environment — whether you can trust the data to be correct, complete, fresh, and available when needed.

The 5 Reliability Characteristics (Motivating Questions)

Motivating QuestionCharacteristicMeaning
After some time, what if the needed data is lost?DurabilityData persists even after system failures
Are we not supposed to get the same data from all copies?ConsistencyAll replicas return the same value
In a team where data changes frequently, don't we want to time travel?Version ControlAbility to access historical snapshots
How fast should data be available for accurate results?PerformanceLow latency access for time-sensitive use cases
As and when we need, is the data available?AvailabilitySystem responds to requests without failure
⚡ Homework Question — WHY is Integrity NOT in the list?
Answer: Integrity is the net result of the other five working correctly. If you have Durability + Consistency + Version Control + Performance + Availability → you have achieved data integrity. It is emergent, not a separate characteristic. This is a commonly asked exam question.

Performance Comparison (Illustration)

Durability (HDFS 3-way replication)95%
Consistency (RDBMS Strong)99%
Availability (NoSQL eventual)85%
Performance (In-memory cache)90%

ACID vs BASE Recap (from Session 1 — used here)

AvailabilityLowerHighly Available
ConsistencyStrong (immediate)Eventual

🔬 Research — The CAP Theorem Connection
The CAP Theorem (Brewer, 2000) states that a distributed system can guarantee at most two of: Consistency, Availability, Partition Tolerance. ACID databases choose C+A (sacrifice P). NoSQL/BASE systems choose A+P (sacrifice strong C). This is the theoretical foundation for why Data Lakes use eventual consistency — partition tolerance and availability matter more at petabyte scale.
Source: Brewer (2000); Gilbert & Lynch, ACM SIGACT, 2002

What is Data Management?

Definition, components, data governance, and data security

📘 Definition
Data Management is the practice of collecting, processing, and using data securely and efficiently for better business outcomes. A strong data management strategy implements four core components to streamline strategy and operations.

📘 Metadata
Metadata = Data about Data. Since data is spread across many systems, you need a repository that tracks WHERE data lives. Like a library index — the catalog is metadata, the books are the data. Activities include: aligning taxonomies across data sources, defining roles and responsibilities for data access.
🔐 Component 4: Data Security+
📘 Definition
Data security sets guardrails in place to protect digital information from unauthorized access, corruption, or theft.

Why it matters: Data loss can be devastating to any business; breaches result in costly financial and brand consequences.

How it's done:

  • Encryption: Data at rest and in transit
  • Data Masking: Hide sensitive fields in views/exports
  • Controlled Access: "Can you see the salary slip of your colleague? Can you see the medical reports of a fellow patient?" → NO. Role-based access (RBAC)
🔬 Research Addition — RBAC in ML Pipelines (2024)
Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) is now extended to ML feature stores and model registries. In platforms like Databricks Unity Catalog, data scientists can access feature tables only if their role grants permission — preventing inadvertent PII leakage into training sets.
Source: Databricks Unity Catalog documentation, 2024

Data Platform

📘 Definition
A Data Platform is a software suite that enables organizations to process and manage large amounts of data quickly and efficiently. It is a central repository and processing house — a collection of data pipelines that ingest, store, transform, and deliver data to various destinations.

Technical requirements include: Fast query processing · Large storage capacity · Elastic computing · In-memory caches · MPP (Massively Parallel Processing) · Columnar storage · Large in-memory data grids and computing clusters

📘 Extraction vs Ingestion — Key Difference
  • Extraction (PULL): You go to the source and pull data manually (e.g., download CSV from Kaggle). Static, point-in-time.
  • Ingestion (ARRIVAL): Data is continuously pushed or streamed into your system via API or pipeline. Dynamic, always up-to-date.
Ingestion = end-to-end process. Extraction = part of ingestion.

Why ETL Has Limitations

QuestionETL Answer
Is our data always structured?❌ No — ETL struggles with semi/unstructured data
Can ETL cater to growing velocity?❌ No — batch processing, not real-time
Can it work with real-time data flow?❌ No — requires pre-defined schema, batch jobs

🌐 Why ELT Emerged

The argument: "Why should I always transform data before loading? Let me load it in whatever form it comes in, and decide later what needs transforming."

Driven by the 5 V's of Big Data (Volume, Velocity, Variety, Veracity, Value) — data was spread across clouds, IoT, and many devices, making pre-transformation impractical.

Data Management · Component 2

Data Storage Architectures

Warehouse vs Lake vs Lakehouse — when to use what

The Core Analogy (Lecture)

🏭 Warehouse Analogy

A finished-goods warehouse (like Amazon fulfillment or Coca-Cola bottling plant) — everything is processed, organized, and ready to dispatch. Highly structured, specific places for everything.

🌊 Lake Analogy

A natural lake — raw, unfiltered water collected from various sources. Unstructured or raw state. Massive scale. Useful when you want to store everything now and figure out what to analyze later.

More examples from lecture:

  • Kitchen cupboard = Data Warehouse (organized, know what's where)
  • Wholesale/cash & carry market = Data Lake (enormous, unquantified items)
  • Ready-made chapati vs. making from scratch = Warehouse vs Lake data

📘 Data Lakehouse
Data Warehouse + Data Lake = Data Lakehouse. Combines the reliability and performance of a warehouse with the flexibility and scale of a data lake. Has schema AND schema-less data in the same system. Example: Delta Lake (Databricks), Apache Iceberg.
🔬 Research Addition — Modern Industry Shift (2024–2025)
The Data Lakehouse architecture has become the dominant paradigm in modern data engineering. Databricks reported that over 60% of their enterprise customers have migrated from separate warehouse+lake setups to a unified Lakehouse by 2024. Apache Iceberg and Delta Lake provide ACID transactions on top of object storage, solving the historic weakness of data lakes (no transactional guarantees).
Source: Databricks State of Data + AI Report, 2024

Generation / Creation

📘 Definition
Data generation or creation is the process of generating or capturing data into some data storage system — but not our own data storage system (it exists at source).

Sources: Devices, Audio messages, Text messages, Images, Videos, Web curation, Log files from servers, Diagnostic data from medical procedures

Data types at this stage: Unstructured · Semi-structured · Structured (all three)

Static vs Frequently Updated Datasets

TypeExampleKey Point
StaticPhoto recognition dataset (months-stable)Works as long as it's representative of current photo types
DynamicSeasonal weather photos (winter only → fails in summer)Distribution shifts over time — needs updating

If outdoor photos represent only winter environments from a temperate climate, the distribution will be significantly diversified when used for summer predictions.

Ingestion — Receiving Data into the System

📘 Key Point
Can we take ALL data created at source? No. A filtering and selection step necessarily occurs. This is inbuilt in ingestion.

Sampling

  • If data volume is too large to process, sampling reduces the load
  • Saves money on intermediate processing and training costs
  • Risk: May occasionally lose detail for some events — unavoidable
  • In general, ML training systems perform better with more data — any reduction may impact quality

⚡ Exam — Extraction vs Ingestion
  • Extraction = PULL — you go and get static data (e.g., download CSV from Kaggle manually). One-time, point-in-time.
  • Ingestion = ARRIVAL — data is forced to arrive via APIs/streaming continuously. Always up-to-date.
  • Ingestion is the end-to-end process; extraction is a part of ingestion.

Processing — Cleaning and Preparing Data

📘 Bucketing
Map a range of data values into a much smaller set of groups that represent the same range. Example: ages 0–17 → "Minor", 18–64 → "Adult", 65+ → "Senior". Reduces cardinality while preserving meaning.
🌐 Why This Matters for ML
Garbage In, Garbage Out (GIGO) — even the most sophisticated model cannot overcome fundamentally bad training data. Validation and cleaning at the processing phase are the last line of defense before data enters the model training pipeline.

⚡ Exam — Visualization Beyond Charts
Visualization in post-processing is NOT just showing a pie chart. It must: (1) explain what each record in the dataset means, (2) show how it links with other datasets, (3) confirm whether it is clean and safe for model training. This is the "data storyboard" concept.
